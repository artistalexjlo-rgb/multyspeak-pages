  • Driving Russian-plated vehicles into Montenegro via EU/Balkan borders (Bulgaria, Baltic states, Turkey) is currently highly restricted or impossible. Authorities often deny entry or transit to vehicles with Russian license plates regardless of the driver's citizenship. It is recommended to sell such vehicles in Russia and purchase a car locally in Montenegro.

  • When driving a rental car across borders (e.g., from Croatia to Montenegro) and the driver is not the registered owner, a power of attorney (доверенность) for the vehicle is generally required, especially if the owner is not present in the car. It's advisable to have this document prepared to avoid potential issues at border control.
  • To avoid potential long waiting times for residency or visa processes in Podgorica, Montenegro, consider exploring options in Sarajevo or Belgrade. These alternative locations may offer a more streamlined procedure.
  • In Montenegro, driving a motorcycle or scooter with an engine capacity over 50cc (like 125cc) requires a category A driver's license. While rental companies may suggest otherwise and police might overlook tourists, it is legally required. Riding a scooter under 50cc is generally permissible with a category B license. If involved in an incident, especially an accident, riding without the correct license can lead to serious legal consequences.
  • When booking accommodation in Montenegro via platforms like Booking.com, the host is obligated to register your stay. While a formal confirmation document is not always provided, having your booking confirmation is generally sufficient for border control. If you wish, you can request a 'potvrda' from the host. The primary requirement for tourists is the booking confirmation itself.
  • There can be a significant temperature difference between the coast and Žabljak in Montenegro. Coastal areas can be hot, while Žabljak, especially in the evenings and at night, can be much cooler with strong winds. It's advisable to pack warmer clothing like sweaters or windbreakers for mountain trips, even during warmer seasons. Daytime can be warm in the sun, but temperatures drop sharply after sunset or when the sun is not present.
  • Foreign tourists in Montenegro must pay a tourist tax and register with local authorities. By law, the accommodation provider is responsible for registration and tax collection. If the host refuses, the tourist must visit the local tourist organization (TO). There is no official fine for tourists for non-payment of the tourist tax, but registration is mandatory. Registration is not required for stays in private property owned by the foreigner or their relatives/friends.
  • For minors (under 18) traveling between Montenegro and Serbia, especially without both parents, it's advisable to check specific airline policies. While Montenegrin border control might not rigorously enforce consent requirements for foreign nationals, some airlines may require a notarized parental consent form ('punomoćje' or 'saglasnost') allowing the minor to travel. In Serbia, individuals aged 16 and above generally do not require parental consent for travel.

While some travelers report regular checks at land borders (e.g., with Serbia), others have experienced no checks at airport departures, as information may be available in digital systems.   • The official tourist tax (boravišna taksa) in Montenegro is 1 EUR per adult per day and 0.5 EUR for minors (12-18). Registration itself is free of charge. Some hotels may attempt to charge extra 'service fees' for registration. Tourists should request an itemized receipt and verify the tax amount against the local tourist board regulations.
  • Importing a drone into Montenegro requires official permission via spiro.medigovic.gov.me. Unauthorized import carries a risk of confiscation or fines at customs. While some travelers report anecdotal success in bringing drones without formal declaration, it remains a legal risk depending on the specific customs officer.
  • In Montenegro, the tourist tax (boravišna taksa) is mandatory for foreigners staying in accommodation. Payment and registration are handled by the accommodation provider. If you stayed in apartments, the host is obligated to register you electronically and collect the tax. You should receive a registration confirmation. If no receipt was provided, you can inquire at local tourist information points or post offices about paying the tax retroactively. While checks at border control are not always stringent, having proof of payment or registration is advisable to avoid potential fines. The registration process is electronic and doesn't typically involve paper slips unless paid directly at a post office or bank.

Tourist tax is mandatory, but local hosts often shift the registration burden to guests. Popular tourism peaks in July; prices for accommodation and transfers increase significantly.
