Shopping in Cuba: what actually matters to know

Real experience of people from chats on «Shopping» in Cuba — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • The Cuban economy faces significant challenges, leading to shortages and difficulties in daily life. State-run stores often require card payments, and even alternative cards like 'Mir' may have inconsistent acceptance. Frequent power outages disrupt electronic transactions. Many hotels do not accept cash. Obtaining local payment cards can be problematic due to personnel shortages. The availability of goods in state-run and tourist-oriented shops is limited, with high prices and often basic items. Private initiatives exist, but prices remain high. Currency exchange rates can be unfavorable. The situation is further complicated by external factors, including sanctions and import restrictions.
  • In Cuba, there is a significant deficit and high price of gasoline due to the socialist economic system. Tour buses are observed operating with very few passengers, consuming fuel inefficiently. There is an unofficial market ('barraging') where drivers sell gasoline, which is more profitable than the official tours. This black market activity is reportedly only possible when supervisory management is not present.
  • In Cuba, payment methods are diverse and vary by establishment type and location. State-owned stores and institutions (e.g., Etecsa for SIM cards in Havana) often do not accept cash (USD, EUR, CUP) for major purchases, preferring bank cards. MIR cards may be accepted but success rates vary by the issuing bank and specific terminal. Private vendors and some state-sector entities in tourist areas (e.g., Varadero) are more likely to accept cash, especially USD, sometimes even informally (transactions 'off the books'). The unofficial exchange rate for USD to CUP can be tracked on sites like ElToque and is significantly higher than the official rate (e.g., 270 to 660 CUP per USD in recent times). It is highly recommended to carry a substantial amount of USD cash. For digital nomads or those with crypto, USDT can be exchanged for local currency with a slight premium (1:1 to 1:1.05), which is advantageous compared to other Latin American countries where a loss of 5% or more is common.
  • In Santa Marta, Cuba, the cost for a round trip via tricycle is approximately 6,000 CUP (approx. $9 USD). Local vendors often speak Russian, and while caution regarding authenticity is advised, some buyers note that the alcohol sold is often genuine or salvaged/pilfered from hotels rather than counterfeit.
  • The 'Mir' payment system is officially functional in Cuba for some retail payments. However, cross-border banking transfers to Cuban-based 'Mir' cards can be inconsistent due to intermediary correspondent banking issues. Peer-to-peer (P2P) exchange remains the most reliable method for travelers and residents to obtain cash in Cuba.
  • As of June 2026, foreign-issued Visa and Mastercard credit and debit cards are blocked for transactions in Cuba. Financial operations for tourists are now almost exclusively dependent on physical cash. Travelers are advised to carry sufficient cash as backup, as reliance on international card payment networks is unreliable or impossible due to US sanctions and local banking restrictions.
  • When traveling to Cuba, consider bringing items like reusable mugs or thermoses, and personal hygiene items. USD is recommended for currency. Turkish Airlines generally offers good onboard catering; expect standard airline meals, potentially with Cuban-influenced dishes on return flights. Small gifts and cosmetics can be appreciated by hotel staff.
  • Cuban cigars, such as Upmann brand, are sought after. Travelers looking to purchase them may need to find them through unofficial channels or arrange for them to be brought back by others. Historically, tourists have also brought items like cosmetics and gifts. Rum is another popular Cuban product.
  • Travelers to Cuba have the option to bring humanitarian aid, with individuals sometimes carrying substantial amounts (e.g., 60 kg) to distribute in cities like Havana. This practice offers a unique way to engage with the local population, providing direct support and gaining a deeper cultural experience beyond traditional tourism.
  • For travelers seeking reliable quality in food and service in Cuba, selecting specific international hotel chains, such as Melia, is recommended. These establishments are generally noted for maintaining higher standards compared to many local options, offering amenities and culinary experiences comparable to those found in other popular tourist destinations like Venezuela.
  • On Cuba, there are reports of hyperinflation starting in May, with official salaries as low as $15/month potentially dropping further. There are unconfirmed rumors about a ban on US dollar circulation, which could make it difficult for tourists. Small businesses might refuse to accept pesos due to rapid devaluation. Electricity outages are frequent, reportedly up to 15 hours per day in Havana. Officially, US dollars are not accepted in 'dollar stores' or most hotels; a local currency called MLC (obtained by exchanging cash dollars at banks) is used. However, some establishments reportedly still accept cash dollars, sometimes preferring it. It's advised for travelers to carry both USD and CUP and be prepared for currency complexities and potential restrictions.
  • A user in Cuba received a bottle of rum as a gift. It was identified as a limited edition dedicated to the 505th anniversary of Havana, purchased from an official store in Havana. This information is useful for rum enthusiasts and collectors.
  • In Cuba, private businesses face significant difficulties obtaining government permits for card payment processing. Consequently, the vast majority of private establishments and many state-owned ones operate exclusively on a cash-only basis, making card terminals an extremely rare sight.
  • Effective June 6, 2026, Cuba will no longer accept Visa and Mastercard for transactions, regardless of the issuing country. This impacts travelers significantly as many establishments, including state-run stores, operate exclusively on card payments. While MIR cards are an alternative, their acceptance can be inconsistent across different banks and locations. Power outages, which are frequent, render all payment terminals inoperable. In some cases, tourists may face difficulties acquiring local Cuban cards due to the unavailability of personnel at service points. This situation poses a risk of being unable to make payments even with cash.
  • Bringing auto parts to Cuba via luggage is possible, but subject to customs regulations. Large quantities, such as 23 kg, may be confiscated. Past experiences include transporting tires without issues. In 2024, clutch cables and small parts were brought in a large bag without problems. Hand luggage is subject to thorough checks. Checked baggage is scanned, and may be subject to additional control if questions arise about the contents, requiring explanations. A persuasive argument might lead to a symbolic customs duty.
  • In Varadero (Cuba), sunbeds are available for rent at approximately 250 CUP per day near 30th Street. The region frequently experiences power outages. While hotels generally maintain electricity, private housing often relies on solar panels for consistent power supply.
  • To purchase luggage in Old Havana: 1. Check the second floor of Centro Comercial Casa Linda on Galliano street, where sets of three suitcases were previously sold. 2. Explore the shops on the first floor of the Habana Libre hotel, specifically the first one to the right upon entering from reception. 3. Consider shops along Obispo Street for offline purchases.
  • When shopping for specific items or luggage in Havana, Cuba, be aware that finding particular items (e.g., motorcycle parts) can be extremely difficult, even with local assistance. Tourist-oriented locations like Calle Obispo and Hotel Packard will have prices 3-4 times higher than in Moscow for similar goods. For better price-to-quality ratio on luggage, consider Mercado Único Cuatro Caminos (near the train station) or Hotel Tryp Habana Libre in Vedado. Online classifieds like Revolico are an option but may involve dealing with unreliable sellers.
