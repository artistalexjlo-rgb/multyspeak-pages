Safety: how it works in Japan — first-hand

Real experience of people from chats on «Safety» in Japan — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • Typhoons in Okinawa are common. Use the NERV app for real-time natural disaster alerts in Japan. Most transport resumes 12-24 hours after a storm passes. Official site for typhoon tracking is the Japan Meteorological Agency.
  • When transporting a MacBook from Japan to Russia via China, consider the following: 1. Personal Use: Carrying the device without its original box and without activating it may help present it as personal use to customs. 2. Tax-Free Purchases: Tax-free refunds are often processed at export. Keep receipts and the item itself for inspection. For electronics like laptops, inquire about specific customs regulations for both export from Japan and import into Russia and China. Some retailers may offer tax-free options, but it's crucial to understand the implications for customs declarations.
  • Bears, particularly brown bears in Hokkaido and Himalayan black bears on Honshu, are present in Japan and pose a potential risk to hikers. Making noise in bear habitats is advised to deter encounters. While bear spray and camping knives are available, their effectiveness against larger bears is debated. Travelers venturing into bear habitats should be aware of safety protocols and consider carrying deterrents.
  • August in Japan is characterized by high temperatures and high humidity (approx 90%). Kyoto experiences particularly high humidity due to its basin location. Travelers are advised to prepare for tropical weather conditions.
  • Japan is widely considered one of the safest countries globally, contrary to some misconceptions fueled by media portrayals. While incidents like sexual harassment ('chikan') or assault can occur, they are rare, and Japan's overall safety record for solo female travelers is exceptionally high compared to many other destinations. Reassurances can include the availability of the Russian embassy contact, potential connections with Russian-speaking residents via language exchange apps, and emphasizing Japan's low crime rates. Fear-mongering narratives are generally unfounded.
  • Japanese souvenir stamps (eki stamp) require separate notebooks from Goshuin (temple seal books). Goshuin are considered sacred and are for temple visits only. For train station/sightseeing stamps, any high-quality notebook works. Umbrellas in communal stands at cafes or shops are often shared, but taking one is a local convention, not a formal right for tourists.
  • Tokyo Disaster Prevention Center provides survival training and education on emergency preparedness (e.g., evacuation kits, stockpile planning). Duration is approximately 1.5–2 hours. Located near shopping centers and transit hubs like monorail lines.
  • First-time travelers to Japan can benefit from advice on customs, currency exchange, transportation, and shopping. Key recommendations include: no liquid restrictions in carry-on, utilizing currency exchange offices like Ninja Exchange for favorable rates, using Google Maps and ICOCA cards for public transport, and choosing appropriate stores for specific purchases (e.g., Don Quijote for sweets, drugstores for cosmetics). Be aware of potential luggage inspections in transit countries.
  • Travelers transiting through Chinese airports after departing Japan should be aware of potential luggage inspections. One traveler reported their checked bag being opened and a power bank for a portable fan being removed, with a note left behind. It is advisable to pack such items in carry-on luggage for easier inspection and to avoid potential confiscation. Customs and immigration checks in Japan were minimal, with no questions asked about receipts, goods, or cash carried.
  • Bear activity in Japan is common in rural and mountain regions. Hikers are advised to purchase and carry bear bells to alert animals of their presence. Local authorities often post signs near areas with recent bear sightings.
  • The Honjo Disaster Prevention Center in Tokyo offers experiential courses on earthquake, flood, and fire safety. Activities include smoke evacuation simulators and seismic vibration platforms. Pre-booking via the official website is recommended. English support is available through staff and translated materials.
  • In Japan, if individual items purchased under tax-free status are under 100,000 JPY, they generally do not require customs declaration upon export, even if carried in hand luggage, provided they are for personal use. The declaration requirement is typically based on the total value of goods and specific item categories, not just the sum of individual tax-free items.
  • In situations where typhoons cause flight cancellations and prevent timely departure from Japan, travelers should be prepared for unforeseen circumstances. It is advisable to obtain official confirmation of flight cancellation from the airline. If visa or permitted stay duration is impacted, contacting Japanese immigration authorities to potentially secure a temporary extension might be necessary. Remaining calm and having contingency plans is crucial.
  • Power bank travel requirements in Japan: ensure the device has clear capacity labeling and (for international transit through China) a CCC mark. Stick to standard 5,000–20,000 mAh capacities to avoid security inspection delays.
  • The limit for exporting cash from Japan is equivalent to 100,000 Japanese Yen per person, regardless of the currency. Travelers are advised to declare amounts exceeding this limit. During departure, customs officials may selectively inquire about the amount of cash carried, especially for those traveling to Russia.
  • Night bus travel in Japan is considered very safe. Luggage is secured in a locked compartment accessible only to the driver. The likelihood of theft inside the bus cabin is extremely low due to surveillance cameras and passenger behavior. Japan's overall safety level is exceptionally high, minimizing concerns about theft during travel.
  • Deer in Nara Park can carry ticks that may transmit Lyme disease (borreliosis). Visitors should check themselves and their clothing thoroughly after visiting the park.
  • During typhoon warnings in Okinawa, transportation services (ferries/flights) are often suspended. Tourists are advised to monitor official weather data via sites like JMA or NERV, stock up on provisions, and verify that their accommodation has a backup power generator in case of utility outages.
  • Early August in Japan is characterized by extreme heat and high humidity. Travelers are advised to use sun umbrellas, portable fans, and maintain hydration. A light layer is necessary indoors, as air conditioning in public transport and shopping centers is set to very low temperatures.
  • Jellyfish activity in Japan peaks in July and August. To ensure safety, swim only within designated swimming zones protected by nets at official public beaches. Stings can be painful and carry risks, especially from box jellyfish. Wearing protective swimming attire is a common practice among locals to prevent skin contact with jellyfish tentacles or residue.
  • Using the Visit Japan Web service with a QR code can expedite immigration and customs clearance upon arrival in Japan. It is recommended to complete the necessary information, including travel details and hotel bookings, in advance. If a hotel is not yet booked, this can be added later. When entering departure cities not listed, manual input may be required. For flights with layovers, use the flight number departing from the layover city. The generated QR code should be saved for use at the airport. Note that this QR code is separate from the tax-free QR code, which is not necessary.
  • Travelers entering Japan are advised to use the 'Visit Japan Web' portal to generate QR codes for immigration and customs. It is possible to register multiple family members under a single primary account by using the 'add family member' function at the end of the registration process. Completing this online saves significant time compared to filling out paper forms on arrival.
  • Shibuya Sky has strict safety rules for the rooftop observation deck: bags, backpacks, caps, and large accessories must be stored in lockers provided on-site. Only mobile phones and cameras with neck straps are permitted on the deck to prevent objects from falling. Locker keys are attached to wristbands.
  • Tipping is not customary in Japan, but giving small gifts (omiage) to hotel or cafe staff as a gesture of appreciation is acceptable. Gifts should be in original, sealed packaging. It is best to present these as regional souvenirs rather than tips, as standard monetary tips may be declined.
  • When transporting electronics like a CD player purchased in Japan, especially a used item without original packaging, it's recommended to pack it securely in checked luggage. Protective measures like wrapping the device in clothing or soft items can help prevent damage during transit. Be aware that new, high-end audio equipment in Japan can be very expensive.
  • Investigate the prevalence of spiders in Okinawa, Japan. Users are concerned about encountering common types like banana spiders. Provide information on the frequency of spider sightings and potential risks to tourists.
