Shopping in Japan: what actually matters to know

Real experience of people from chats on «Shopping» in Japan — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • Tax-free in Japan is handled at the point of sale. You must present your passport at the register. The store clerk will process the documentation, and the tax will either be deducted from the price immediately, refunded in cash, or processed through a specific app. You cannot claim tax refunds at the airport upon departure for items purchased online or at local shops.
  • For purchasing used electronics in Japan, Hard Off stores are recommended. The Hard Off Sakai Shinkanaoka branch in Osaka is highlighted for its good prices and selection. Generally, stores located further from tourist areas tend to offer better value.
  • ICOCA/Suica cards have a maximum balance limit of 20,000 JPY. Cards can be recharged multiple times after the balance is spent. They are widely accepted in convenience stores (konbini) and chain restaurants, serving as a supplement to cash for tourists without international bank cards.
  • When transporting a MacBook from Japan to Russia via China, consider the following: 1. Personal Use: Carrying the device without its original box and without activating it may help present it as personal use to customs. 2. Tax-Free Purchases: Tax-free refunds are often processed at export. Keep receipts and the item itself for inspection. For electronics like laptops, inquire about specific customs regulations for both export from Japan and import into Russia and China. Some retailers may offer tax-free options, but it's crucial to understand the implications for customs declarations.
  • In Japan, tax-free shopping usually requires scanning the entry stamp in a physical passport. However, some stores support scanning a QR code generated via the 'Visit Japan Web' service. This is not universally supported by all retailers, so retailers' policies may vary.
  • For currency exchange in Japan: 1. Okinawa: Use Bank of Okinawa exchangers, often found in FamilyMart, which accept USD. 2. Tokyo: Exchange rates vary; check arrival terminals (e.g., 153.73 JPY at international arrivals). 3. Asakusa/Kamakura/Kyoto: Look for reputable exchangers like 'Ninja exchanger' during business hours. 4. Payment methods: While UnionPay is not universally accepted (e.g., 7-Eleven), it works at Lawson, hotels, and taxis. Be prepared for 'cash only' establishments. Consider carrying sufficient cash.
  • When traveling to Japan, travelers may need voltage converters or power adapters. Japan uses 100V, and outlets are Type A (two flat pins). Devices designed for 220-240V may require a voltage converter. Adapters may be needed for plugs with different configurations. Some electronic stores in Akihabara may sell these items, or travelers can research specific needs for their devices.
  • Japan uses Type A (two-pin non-polarized) plugs, 100V. Most modern mobile and laptop power bricks support 100-240V, meaning no voltage transformer is required, only a physical plug adapter. High-power appliances (e.g., Dyson hair dryers) may require a step-up voltage transformer.
  • Starting from iOS 16, the Japanese iPhone camera shutter sound is location-dependent and disables automatically outside Japan (upon changing geo/SIM). Apple Stores in Japan no longer offer Tax Free. New models (Pro/Pro Max) are rarely available in stock during release seasons due to high reseller demand. Japan-market models (starting from iPhone 17) may transition exclusively to dual eSIMs.
  • First-time travelers to Japan can benefit from advice on customs, currency exchange, transportation, and shopping. Key recommendations include: no liquid restrictions in carry-on, utilizing currency exchange offices like Ninja Exchange for favorable rates, using Google Maps and ICOCA cards for public transport, and choosing appropriate stores for specific purchases (e.g., Don Quijote for sweets, drugstores for cosmetics). Be aware of potential luggage inspections in transit countries.
  • Japanese sunscreen (SPF 50+) is widely available in drugstores like Cocokarafine or 7-Eleven, typically priced at 800–1500 JPY, but usually comes in small containers (approx. 100ml). Specialized after-sun care (like Panthenol-based products) is rare in Japanese pharmacies. Tourists should bring their own soothing creams for sunburns, as local pharmacy alternatives are either limited to aloe gels or tiny therapeutic ointments.
  • For visitors to Japan, Kyoto is recommended for historical sites and atmosphere, best visited early morning. Osaka is preferred for nightlife, modern shopping, and as a central hub for day trips to Nara, Kyoto, and Kobe.
  • For portable appliances like Dyson hair dryers, travelers may need a voltage converter if their device operates on a higher voltage (e.g., 220-240V) than Japan's standard 100V. A travel-specific model like the Dyson Supersonic Travel is designed for portability and may negate the need for a separate converter. Alternatively, purchasing a local appliance might be more practical.
  • ATB UnionPay cards work in Japan but may require specific ATM handling. If a 4-digit PIN is not accepted, try adding two zeros at the end of the code (6-digit format). When paying at stores, if the card is declined, inform staff it is 'Chugoku no kado' (China UnionPay) to ensure they select the correct payment terminal mode.
  • Russian-issued cards (specifically UnionPay) have limited acceptance in Japan; they work for cash withdrawals at 7-11 ATMs and some major retail stores but are unreliable for general contactless payments. Russian travel aggregators (Ostrovok, Trip.com) accept Russian-issued cards for hotel and transport bookings.
  • Russian Agricultural Bank (RSXHB) UnionPay cards are effectively non-functional in Japan due to sanctions. Offline payments and ATM withdrawals are consistently declined. ATB (Automated Trading Bank) cards are currently cited as the only reliable option for Russian citizens in Japan.
  • Tax-free refunds in Japan (e.g., Uniqlo) are technically possible for already purchased items. The process usually requires the passport used for the original purchase, the receipt, and the item(s). Some stores may require returning all items from the transaction and performing a new sale. Note that some stores explicitly state that all tax-free purchases are final, so individual store policy prevails.

  • In addition to Tax-Free refunds, tourists can get extra discounts in stores like Don Quijote or Big Camera using apps like 'Payke' or 'JapanTravel (Navitime)'. These typically provide up to 5-7% off for tourists; users must present their passport and the app coupon at checkout.
  • In Japan, if individual items purchased under tax-free status are under 100,000 JPY, they generally do not require customs declaration upon export, even if carried in hand luggage, provided they are for personal use. The declaration requirement is typically based on the total value of goods and specific item categories, not just the sum of individual tax-free items.
  • Key travel insights from Japan: Currency exchange is best at dedicated exchange offices like Ninja Exchange in Shinjuku for optimal rates. For shopping, Don Quijote is good for sweets and alcohol, Tom's for cosmetics, and Ginza Karen for luggage. Google Maps is highly recommended for navigation. Notably, there are no restrictions on liquids in carry-on baggage at Japanese airports, allowing for the transport of items like alcohol. Tax-free shopping procedures and purchasing electronics are also mentioned.
  • Duty-free items purchased in Japanese stores (Don Quijote, etc.) are sealed in special bags. These bags must not be opened until leaving the country. Regulations are expected to change significantly as of November 2026, which will affect the current tax-free shopping system.
  • In Japan, tax-free purchases are categorized into general goods (clothing, electronics) and consumable goods (cosmetics, food, drugs). Consumables are often sealed in clear anti-tamper bags which technically should not be opened until leaving the country. General goods do not require such sealing. Customs checks on these bags at departure are rare for tourists unless quantities are suspicious (bulk/resale). Most travelers open packages for luggage optimization without consequences.

  • Bic Camera is recommended as a more organized alternative to Don Quijote for last-minute shopping in Japan. It stocks a wide variety of goods, including electronics, cosmetics, food items, and souvenirs, with better customer service and less crowding.
  • Eating and drinking on Shinkansen (bullet trains) in Japan is not only acceptable but common. Bento boxes and beverages, including alcoholic options like beer, sake, and imported wines, are readily available for purchase at stations and on the train. While bringing your own alcohol is generally fine, purchasing it onboard is a convenient option.
  • For technical literature in Japan, consider large bookstores like Maruzen, which often have extensive sections for specialized academic and scientific fields. University libraries and campus bookstores are also excellent resources for niche publications. Some may offer limited access or the ability to photograph specific pages.
  • When purchasing electronics in Japan, look for tax-free options. Items like iPhones, headphones, and watches may be available. Check store websites for availability, and items can sometimes be transferred between stores.
  • Travelers in Tokyo looking for affordable luggage often find deals in Asakusa (near Senso-ji Temple) and Akihabara. Typical prices for M-size luggage range from 3,500 to 5,000 JPY, with L-size around 6,900 JPY.
  • Inquire about the cost-effectiveness of purchasing Apple products in Japan compared to other regions, such as Russia. Research current pricing and potential tax differences.
  • During typhoon warnings in Okinawa, transportation services (ferries/flights) are often suspended. Tourists are advised to monitor official weather data via sites like JMA or NERV, stock up on provisions, and verify that their accommodation has a backup power generator in case of utility outages.
  • Don Quijote (Donki) stores in Japan are large-scale discount chains, often operating 24/7. They function as a 'one-stop' shop selling a wide range of goods, including cosmetics, groceries, electronics, luggage, and novelty items. Many locations in tourist areas (like Shinjuku or Asakusa) maintain 24-hour service, making them convenient for late-night shopping.
  • Early August in Japan is characterized by extreme heat and high humidity. Travelers are advised to use sun umbrellas, portable fans, and maintain hydration. A light layer is necessary indoors, as air conditioning in public transport and shopping centers is set to very low temperatures.
  • Japan is a hybrid economy. While many shops, cafes (Starbucks Shibuya), and transport services are cashless-only or prefer IC cards (Suica/Pasmo), traditional local businesses and vending machines still favor or exclusively accept cash. Travelers should carry both a reliable foreign-issued card (or UnionPay like ATB) and Japanese yen banknotes for emergencies.
  • While Japan is increasingly cashless, cash is still required for some small shops, temples, and local buses. Travelers are advised to carry 10,000 JPY in cash as a backup. IC cards (Suica, Pasmo, etc.) are highly recommended for transport and occasional small payments.
  • Welcome Suica: Free to issue, non-refundable, expires after 28 days. Best for short-term tourists. Standard Suica: Costs 500 JPY deposit, refundable, valid for 10 years from last use. Both act as digital wallets for transit and convenience stores.
  • While some shops seal Tax-Free clothing in restricted bags, Japanese customs rarely inspect these items at departure. Tax-Free status is generally granted to tourists for items exceeding 5000 JPY.
