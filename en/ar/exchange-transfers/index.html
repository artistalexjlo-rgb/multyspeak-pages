Exchange & transfers in Argentina: what actually matters to know

Real experience of people from chats on «Exchange & transfers» in Argentina — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • Private Argentine banks (such as Galicia and Santander) strictly regulate foreign inbound transfers and may freeze funds sent by third parties (non-relatives). For direct international bank transfers (e.g., from Kazakhstan), using the state-owned Banco de la Nación Argentina (BNA) is more reliable. For crypto-to-fiat transactions, the local app 'Belo' is highly recommended as it issues a usable local debit card. The Dollar App (ARQ) accepts SWIFT transfers but acts as a foreign card in Argentina, meaning funds can be spent digitally but not easily withdrawn as cash.
  • For favorable Euro to Argentine Peso exchange rates in Palermo, Buenos Aires, consider using unofficial exchange services like 'Rebi', which also handles cryptocurrency. Contact them via Telegram to arrange a meeting and exchange. While the rate might be only slightly lower than the best market rate, access to these services may require prior arrangement.
  • In Argentina, card payments are widely accepted, but often with unfavorable exchange rates. It is advisable to carry US dollars or cryptocurrency and exchange them for local pesos upon arrival. Currently, 20,000 peso banknotes are in circulation. While dollars may be accepted in some central areas of Buenos Aires, their acceptance is less common in other regions, and the exchange rate might be less advantageous. Outside the capital, having Argentine pesos in cash is essential as card acceptance is limited and exchange offices may be scarce.
  • Tinkoff Bank's security algorithms may flag and block international transfers, even for first-time users with no outstanding loans. Expect inquiries regarding the recipient's identity, relationship, purpose of the transfer, and communication methods. These checks are related to currency regulations. Transfers through alternative services like Ozon may encounter fewer restrictions.
  • Brazilian Reals can be exchanged for Argentine Pesos in Buenos Aires, particularly on Florida Street and in tourist areas like San Telmo. In some tourist-centric locations, Brazilian Reals may even be accepted directly as a form of payment.
  • Cryptocurrency is the most efficient and cost-effective method for transferring funds to Argentina. Users are advised to utilize verified exchange bots such as 'cashar' for local P2P transactions.
  • Crypto-to-fiat conversion in Buenos Aires is primarily conducted via private offline exchanges (cuevas). Reputable exchanges do not advertise with signs; finding them usually requires specific Telegram bots or community recommendations. Bybit Pay is a cited method for online transactions.
  • For carrying physical cash, discreet running belts worn under clothing are recommended for security. When moving to Argentina, relying on P2P or local exchangers carries significant fraud risks, especially when dealing with strangers in private messages. Always prioritize established exchange offices (cuevas) or peer-to-peer platforms with high trust ratings if banking options are restricted.
  • Commonly used financial services in Argentina include: Galicia (bank), Brubank (user-friendly), Cuenta DNI (discounts on food/services), Mercado Pago (standard payments), Naranja (credit card), and Ripio (crypto/cashback). Santander may require specific authorizations for international/dollar transactions.
  • In Argentina, landlords and local exchange offices (cuevas) often reject USD bills with small marks, stamps, or ink writings, or accept them at a steep discount. To solve this: 1) If the client has a local USD bank account (e.g., Santander or Galicia), they can deposit the marked bills via ATM or cashier (caja de ahorro en dólares), and withdraw them later to receive clean bills. 2) Alternatively, some exchange services like 'Optima' accept damaged or marked bills with a relatively small discount of around 2.5%.
  • In Buenos Aires, currency exchange offices (cuevas) and Western Union branches are available on Saturdays, though they typically open later in the morning and have shorter operating hours compared to weekdays.
  • For collectors seeking older, low-denomination Argentine peso banknotes (often featuring animal motifs), banks and currency exchange offices are ineffective as they primarily handle high-denomination, current-issue currency. Local street markets ('ferias') and antique shops are the recommended venues to find discontinued or specific currency issues in good condition.
  • Withdrawing cash from foreign bank cards via local ATMs in Argentina involves high fees. The recommended alternative is using peer-to-peer (P2P) crypto exchanges (USDT) or private money changers operating via Telegram to convert funds into Argentine pesos. Transaction losses via these methods are typically around 4%.

  • In the Argentine side of Iguazu, currency exchange (Reals to Argentine Pesos) is available at "Casa de cambio" locations. These typically open between 9-10 AM; early morning exchanges (e.g., 7 AM) are unlikely. Be cautious of counterfeit 20-peso notes; genuine notes reportedly do not react to a counterfeit detection marker, while fakes might. Shops may also exchange currency at a less favorable rate. For purchases, paying with a credit card is an option (be aware of potential commissions), and U.S. dollars might be accepted at a poor to average exchange rate for souvenirs.
  • Transferring funds from Kazakhstan to Argentina via Western Union might be more cost-effective than direct ATM withdrawals. However, ensure your bank allows such international transfers as a non-resident. Be aware of Western Union's transaction limits, which can restrict transfers exceeding a certain amount (e.g., a few thousand USD). Issues with blocked transfers and subsequent resolution difficulties have been reported.
  • Feasibility: It is possible to exchange cryptocurrency for cash in Argentina without a local DNI (Argentine ID). Requirement: A passport (foreign passport) can be used for the exchange. Limitation: Not all exchange points or services will accept a passport; users may need to find specific providers.
  • To exchange Euros in Buenos Aires: You can exchange at banks, but check the rates. Exchange offices (casas de cambio) often offer better rates. 'Cambio Baires' is a recommended exchange office. As of recent information, the exchange rate for EUR to ARS was around 1.206 (this may refer to a specific currency pair or unofficial market rate, so always verify locally).
  • Paying via Sberbank QR in Argentina through Mercado Pago is unreliable. Users report consistent failures regardless of app version or device (Android/iOS). When cross-border QR payments do function, they often incur significant exchange rate losses (approx 8-10%). Cash exchange at local exchange houses ('cuevas') typically offers a more favorable rate compared to automated banking channels.
  • In Buenos Aires, the 'Cashar' app is used to compare exchange rates and locate exchange points (cuevas). P2P crypto-to-cash services exist where couriers deliver currency in exchange for crypto transfers on the spot.
  • Directly withdrawing PayPal USD to Argentine bank accounts is highly problematic. Local exchange services (cuevas) can cash out PayPal funds to ARS or USD cash, but they charge a high commission fee ranging from 4% to 10%.
  • Using Belarusian Visa or Mastercard in Argentina can be problematic. Major supermarkets such as Coto, Disco, Jumbo, and Dia often do not accept them. Withdrawing cash is also difficult. Exchange offices frequently require a DNI, even for currency exchange. Farmacity has also been reported to refuse transactions with non-Argentinian bank cards. It is suggested to seek out Russian-speaking exchange offices, as they may process transactions without a DNI. Another option is to transfer Belarusian rubles to a Russian bank account and then withdraw funds in Argentina. Ensure you have alternative payment methods or sufficient local currency.
  • Patagonia in winter (June) is off-season but fully operational for parks and trekking. Windy conditions are less frequent in winter. In Buenos Aires, old-series USD (2006) are accepted at par. Currency exchange in Iguazu often incurs a discount. Use 'Patagonia.chic' for accurate hourly weather forecasts.
  • For travelers in Argentina, carrying physical USD cash to exchange locally is recommended to obtain more favorable exchange rates compared to international card transactions.
  • For individuals without an Argentine DNI, withdrawing cash via ATMs is difficult due to high fees. An alternative is to request cash back at checkout in major supermarket chains (Coto, Carrefour, Disco) or pharmacies (Farmacity) by asking 'Quiero retirar efectivo'. Success depends on whether the card BIN is recognized as credit or debit.
