Transport: how it works in Sri Lanka — first-hand

Real experience of people from chats on «Transport» in Sri Lanka — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • There is a direct highway express bus from Matara to Colombo Bandaranaike International Airport (BIA). The journey takes approximately 3 hours (e.g., departure at 13:30 arrives around 16:30). The bus drops passengers off a 10-15 minute walk from the airport terminal (a tuk-tuk for this short remaining distance costs around 800 LKR). This bus runs strictly via the highway and does not stop in coastal towns like Unawatuna.
  • Taxi rates from Colombo Airport (CMB) to southern destinations (like Unawatuna or Mirissa) typically range from 11,000 to 15,000 LKR via PickMe. You can negotiate directly with drivers at the departures area to lower the price to 9,000–12,000 LKR. Alternatively, budget travelers can take an express bus to Colombo Fort (300 LKR), a train to Galle (approx. 900 LKR), and a local bus to Unawatuna (approx. 80 LKR).
  • Taxi drivers using ride-hailing apps like Pick Me Up or Uber in Sri Lanka may ask passengers to cancel the ride in the app to avoid paying the service commission (which can range from 7% to 20%). This practice removes the trip from the passenger's app history, making it difficult to resolve potential disputes. Passengers are strongly advised not to comply with such requests. If cancellation is unavoidable, take a screenshot of the trip details (cost, route) beforehand. For cash payments, the driver is responsible for remitting the commission to the service.
  • Not all trains passing through Ella cross the Nine Arch Bridge. The route between Badulla and Ambewela is currently active. For the best views towards Ella, sit on the left side of the train. Tickets for the 2nd class should be purchased at the station approximately one hour before departure, as window seats are highly competitive.
  • Tourists can obtain a temporary Sri Lankan driving permit at Bandaranaike International Airport (Colombo) near the exit, right behind the currency exchange counters. The cost is 15,000 LKR, and you must present your national driving license. If you attempt to access this counter after leaving the arrival hall, security may block you unless you pay 500 LKR for an entry ticket or state that you only need to access the currency exchange.
  • Colombo Bandaranaike International Airport (CMB) arrival logistics: Currency exchange and SIM card kiosks are located after baggage claim. Basic seating is available in the arrival hall. For long layovers before check-in, suggested strategies include pre-booking early hotel arrival or purchasing a day-pass/guest-access at a resort.
  • Top locations for digital nomads in Sri Lanka: Southern coast (Hikkaduwa, Unawatuna, Weligama, Mirissa, Ahangama, Kabalana) and Eastern coast (Nilaveli, Trincomalee, Arugam Bay). Infrastructure: Colombo is the only place with condos. For private houses (villas), availability is similar across coastal towns. Seasonal strategy: spend ~6 months in the South and move to the East or center during the off-season. Scooter rental is common for long-term stays.
  • For Aeroflot flights departing Colombo (Sri Lanka), an additional 23kg baggage allowance costs approximately 170 USD (as of recent reports). Six months prior, it was 150 USD. One user also mentioned 150 EUR. Payment is typically made at the check-in counter, often in USD or EUR.
  • When arriving at Sri Lankan airports, pre-booked transfers are generally the most cost-effective option. While services like PickMe are functional and a reasonable choice, Uber can be significantly more expensive for airport rides. Taxis directly from the airport are typically the priciest transportation option during a stay, but may be necessary if other arrangements aren't made in advance.
  • Driving in Sri Lanka requires adherence to local regulations regarding driving licenses. While International Driving Permits (IDP) are generally recognized, there can be discrepancies based on the convention under which the IDP was issued (Geneva 1949 vs. Vienna 1968). Sri Lanka officially operates under the Geneva Convention, but the Motor Traffic Act (Section 132b) mandates obtaining a 'Recognition Permit' based on your foreign license, which can be processed at the airport or a local office for a fee (approx. 15,000 LKR). Some travelers report successfully using their IDP by referencing the law or by paying a small 'fine' to the police if stopped. However, for insurance purposes in case of an accident, having the official Recognition Permit is crucial. Carrying an IDP issued under the Vienna Convention might lead to complications. It's advisable to consult pinned messages in relevant travel groups or local forums for the most current advice.
  • The most widely used and reliable taxi applications in Sri Lanka are 'PickMe' and 'Uber'. For long-distance travel, private pre-booked transfer services are recommended.
  • Car rental in Sri Lanka: 1. IDP (International Driving Permit) is usually sufficient, but local conversion is recommended to avoid police friction. 2. Fuel: Fuel stations often use a QR-code system for locals, but tourists can usually get full tanks without it. 3. Deposit: Expect a deposit of $200–$400; damages are typically deducted from this (approx. $100 per scratch). 4. Traffic: Buses have right-of-way by force. Driving in major cities like Kandy or Colombo is extremely challenging due to unpredictable motorcycle/tuk-tuk traffic.
  • Operating a motorcycle in Sri Lanka requires a local license with category A. Foreign driving licenses with category B only are insufficient. Local police enforcement results in fines ranging from 5,000 to 15,000 LKR for riding without the proper category.
  • For long-distance taxi services or multi-day private drivers in Sri Lanka, it is recommended to first research current market prices for the desired routes. Then, negotiate with hotel managers, clearly stating your budget expectations based on your research. This method can help secure reliable drivers at fair prices. Direct recommendations of specific individuals are often discouraged due to varying service quality.
  • Tourists can obtain a temporary local driving permit at Bandaranaike International Airport (CMB). The counter is located on the left side upon exit. The cost is approximately 15,000 LKR for a one-month permit, and the process takes about 20 minutes without queues. Police stops for bikers are common. Some tourists report resolving issues with a small payment (e.g., 2000 LKR) or by confidently driving past if they possess a permit.
  • A microbus service operates daily from Ella to Arugam Bay, departing at 9 AM from the main bus station. The cost for a solo traveler is 5000 LKR. This service also offers routes to southern destinations like Unawatuna and Hikkaduwa. Contact details may be found by asking at the main bus stop.
  • Sri Lanka has some of Asia's most scenic railway routes, primarily in the central highlands (e.g., Badulla-Ella). Coastal routes like Unawatuna-Galle are short (approx. 15 minutes) and less scenic. Trains, especially on popular routes during peak hours or weekends, can be very crowded, with passengers often standing or sitting on the floor. Traveling with infants or small children on such routes may not be comfortable due to overcrowding.
  • Soursop (graviola) is highly perishable and difficult to transport. Unripe fruits often fail to ripen properly post-flight, while ripe ones rot quickly. The best practice is to ask the local seller to select fruits specifically for export/flight; they can identify the exact maturity level needed to survive the journey and ripen safely at home.
  • In Sri Lanka, street tuk-tuk drivers routinely overcharge tourists, asking for LKR 500-600 for trips that cost LKR 300. Use the PickMe app to check the fair local price, then negotiate with street drivers using this exact rate as your target. Since PickMe drivers often cancel or fail to arrive, this hybrid negotiation method works best. Exchange phone numbers with reliable drivers for future direct bookings.
  • In highly popular tourist locations on Sri Lanka (such as Coconut Tree Hill in Mirissa), local tuk-tuk drivers ('tuk-tuk mafia') may physically intercept or intimidate app-based PickMe drivers, forcing them to cancel the ride or park far away. To avoid this, walk 300–500 meters away from the local tuk-tuk stands before ordering a ride through PickMe or Uber.
  • To travel from Colombo Airport (CMB) to Trincomalee in Sri Lanka, alternatives to expensive taxis ($100) include: taking a train or a night bus with air conditioning originating from Colombo. For return trips from Trincomalee to the airport, travelers can seek out others to share transfer costs.
  • Motorbike rental (e.g., Royal Enfield 350cc) averages around 45 USD/day in Sri Lanka. Essential gear for motorcycle travelers: high-quality rain gear (bring from home, as local options are often poor quality) and reflective gear for night driving due to poor road lighting.
  • Tuk-tuk rental is possible through direct negotiations with local drivers. It is a manual transmission vehicle. Tourists driving tuk-tuks are subject to more frequent police stops compared to motorcycle riders. Rental prices for vehicles are negotiable.
  • Online train ticket availability in Sri Lanka for routes like Colombo-Ella is often misleading or limited. Tickets can typically be purchased in person at the Colombo Fort station on the day of departure. Verify official operational status through reliable travel resources rather than relying on outdated forum rumors about infrastructure damage.
  • International Driving Permits (IDP) are recognized in Sri Lanka. If an IDP is not available, a temporary local driving permit can be obtained at the airport for approximately $50 USD. While official regulations exist, practical situations can vary. For stays exceeding a year, specific driving license validity requirements may apply.

Average driving speed is 30-40 km/h. Guesthouses can often be booked on Booking.com without prepayment. Regarding valuables, there are reports of theft from items left in vehicles with drivers. It is strongly advised not to leave large sums of money in the car; passports and laptops might be safer but carrying money on your person is recommended to mitigate risks.

  • In Sri Lanka, there is no commonly known '19 km/h grace' for speeding fines. While tourists might not always be strictly monitored for minor speeding due to road conditions limiting speed, traffic laws are in place. A significant rule change/enforcement update was implemented around April-May, making seatbelt usage strictly enforced. Fines can be issued for both the driver and the front-seat passenger if not wearing a seatbelt. Tour guides are generally aware of these stricter enforcement measures. Speed limits are indicated, especially on toll roads.
  • In Sri Lanka, popular ride-hailing applications for transportation include 'PickMe' and 'Uber'. These apps can be used to book taxis and other forms of transport.
  • Recommended night transport from Bandaranaike International Airport (CMB) to Colombo city center is a taxi via 'PickMe' or 'Uber' apps. Public buses (e.g., #187) are available but less convenient at night due to arrival times and lack of direct drop-off at specific hotel locations. Estimated taxi fare is approximately 8-15 EUR. Avoid tuk-tuks for long-distance airport transfers, especially at night.
  • Sri Lankan trains offer both reserved and unreserved tickets. 1st class always requires booking in advance and has closed windows due to AC. 2nd and 3rd classes usually feature open doors and windows. Note that 2nd class has two separate schemes: unreserved (cheap, tickets are always available, but you might have to stand) and reserved (guarantees a specific seat, must be booked in advance).

  • Fuel is readily available for tourists in Weligama, Sri Lanka. While prices may have increased slightly, tourists can purchase gasoline without queues or QR codes. This indicates a supportive approach by local authorities towards the tourism sector, ensuring convenient access to fuel for visitors.
  • Sri Lankan expressways (highways) strictly prohibit motorcycles and three-wheelers (tuk-tuks). Access is restricted to 4-wheeled vehicles only. Navigation apps often suggest longer alternative routes for bikers to avoid these highways.
  • To legally drive a scooter/motorcycle in Sri Lanka, an International Driving Permit (IDP) with category A is generally required. Additionally, as of August 2025, a local temporary permit is also needed, which can be obtained upon arrival at the airport. Driving without a valid category A license (or IDP/local permit) can result in fines ranging from 1,000 to 25,000 LKR (approx. 3-80 USD). The owner of the rental bike may also face fines. Renters often take a deposit to cover potential penalties. While official fines are high, many situations are resolved on the spot with a payment of 2,000-5,000 LKR. Police stops for tourists are common.
  • In Sri Lanka, a local or international driving license (Category A) is not required for renting and operating scooters with an engine capacity of 49cc or less (e.g., Honda Dio models). These vehicles are typically automatic. Rental availability, condition, and pricing might vary, with potentially fewer and poorer quality options on the East Coast compared to the South.
