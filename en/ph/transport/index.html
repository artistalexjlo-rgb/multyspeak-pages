Transport in Philippines: what actually matters to know

Real experience of people from chats on «Transport» in Philippines — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • When flying to the Philippines, an onward ticket or proof of departure is often required, especially when flying from countries like Thailand. Using a legitimate onward ticket service with a verifiable PNR is recommended. While some users have successfully flown without being asked for proof of onward travel, it's safer to have a confirmed booking. The requirement is typically checked during check-in at the departure country.
  • Inter-island ferry services like 2GO offer travel between major Philippine islands. A trip from Manila to Cebu costs approximately 3400 PHP and takes about 12 hours (e.g., 20:30 to 09:30). Tickets can be purchased via their mobile app or at port ticket counters. Baggage allowance is generous (up to 50 kg per person with 2GO, often not strictly weighed). Simple dinner and breakfast are usually included in the fare. Other routes, such as Liloan (Cebu) to Siquijor, are also available with specific schedules. Ferries are a viable alternative to flights, especially for travelers with significant luggage or those seeking a different travel experience.
  • Moalboal (Cebu) is highly recommended for snorkeling, offering accessible marine life like sardines and turtles. For logistics between Cebu, Bohol, and Panglao, ferries are more efficient and reliable than local flights. Renting a motorbike is the preferred method of transport for island exploration.
  • Transportation from Cebu to El Nido primarily involves flights. Minivans are often available to connect with flight schedules for travel to El Nido. Direct night transport options are limited. Travel time from Cebu to Moalboal by taxi can exceed 2.5 hours.
  • Road travel in the Philippines (especially on Cebu) is slow and congested with tricycles, pedicabs, and livestock. Speed limits are typically 30-40 km/h in towns and rarely exceed 60 km/h on highways. For tourists, hiring a private driver is often more efficient and less stressful than self-driving.
  • While technically a Category A license is required for motorcycles/scooters in the Philippines, many rental agencies, especially in tourist areas, do not strictly enforce this for foreigners. Renters often only require basic personal information (name, phone, hotel) and a valid ID, sometimes accepting photos of licenses. However, a valid Category A license is crucial in case of an accident or serious police check. Local police have been reported to show leniency towards tourists, sometimes waving them through checkpoints without full document inspection.
  • Motorcycle rental and speed limits in the Philippines. General consensus is that there are no strict speed limits and it's up to the rider's discretion, but official limits are 30-40 km/h in populated areas and up to 60 km/h outside. Cameras are present, especially in large cities. Locals can be unpredictable, so caution is advised.
  • Russian driving licenses are valid in the Philippines for up to 90 days from the date of entry. This is due to Russia being a signatory to international conventions that facilitate the recognition of domestic driving permits. Renting a car and interactions with local police without an International Driving Permit (IDP) have been reported as uneventful within this 90-day period.
  • To visit multiple islands in the Philippines like Boracay, Cebu, Bohol, Coron, Palawan, or El Nido, you can utilize ferries and domestic flights. Ferry tickets can often be purchased closer to the travel date, but booking in advance is recommended for popular routes or during peak season. Domestic flight tickets should ideally be booked in advance, especially for popular routes or if seeking better prices. Consider the logistical order of islands to minimize travel time and cost. Research specific ferry operators (e.g., OceanJet, SuperCat) and airlines (e.g., Cebu Pacific, Philippine Airlines) for booking options and schedules.
  • For a two-week trip to the Philippines, a combination of Boracay and Panglao is recommended for first-time visitors. Boracay offers excellent beaches, a long coastline, and diverse dining options, suitable for a week-long stay. Panglao and Bohol also feature beautiful beaches and attractions like the Chocolate Hills, butterfly museums, and tarsiers. July is generally not peak season due to potential rain and storms, with September and October posing higher weather risks. For diving enthusiasts, consider Donsol, Subic Bay, Puerto Galera, Palawan, Malapascua, and Panglao. El Nido is beautiful but can be crowded and expensive for a first visit. Direct flights from Hong Kong may not be available; connecting flights are more likely. When choosing between Manila and Cebu for island hopping, consider Palawan is closer to Manila, while Bohol is closer to Cebu. Consider Siargao for beginner surfing, with options for excursions to nearby islands. Expect varied prices across islands; for example, beachfront hotels in Boracay might cost around $46, while similar accommodation in Palawan or El Nido could be around $180. For a shorter trip, consider combining Palawan and El Nido. For diving, Napaling reef and Balicasag are options in Panglao, with sardines typically found at Panagsama.
