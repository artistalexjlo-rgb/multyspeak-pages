Safety in Philippines: what actually matters to know

Real experience of people from chats on «Safety» in Philippines — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • Russian driving licenses are valid in the Philippines for up to 90 days from the date of entry. This is due to Russia being a signatory to international conventions that facilitate the recognition of domestic driving permits. Renting a car and interactions with local police without an International Driving Permit (IDP) have been reported as uneventful within this 90-day period.
  • Exporting corals from the Philippines may be restricted. Check current regulations for protected species and export permits before attempting to transport corals.
  • For parents traveling with young children (ages 5, 8, and 10), there's a conscious effort to balance experiencing local life and culture with safety. While understanding different lifestyles is important for children, nighttime excursions are avoided due to safety concerns. Daytime exploration with explanations is preferred.
  • In the Philippines, local rum is typically made from sugar cane using established technology, making it generally safe to consume. Mixing rum with cola is a common practice, often done to make a few bottles last longer for a group, reflecting a social custom rather than a desire to avoid intoxication.
  • Philippine culinary traditions include a cooking style where food is prepared until 'burnt,' applied to meats, sausages, and chicken. This is considered a cultural practice. Hairstyling for women often involves straightening hair without adding volume, as voluminous styles are not considered fashionable. Furthermore, homosexual relationships are accepted and supported by society and parents.
  • The Philippines is considered a prime destination for diving, with some arguing that its less developed infrastructure enhances the diving experience compared to overly serviced locations like Turkey or Miami. However, safety in diving requires extensive training, including theoretical knowledge and practical skills in both pool and open water environments, preparing for challenging conditions like storms and currents. Simply obtaining a certificate without adequate preparation is strongly discouraged due to high risks.
  • While whale sharks are a sought-after attraction in Palawan, sightings are not guaranteed. In areas like Sabang, the presence of dangerous wildlife, such as saltwater crocodiles, is also a concern. These large reptiles are known to occasionally inhabit coastal areas. Local accounts often emphasize the significant danger posed by crocodiles, with vivid descriptions of encounters.
  • When exploring Manila with a child, it's advisable to be aware of safety. Areas like BGC are considered safe to walk around even late at night. However, central Manila may have a higher presence of beggars and potentially unsafe individuals, even during the day. For currency exchange, rates may be better on the second floor of the airport compared to other areas.
  • Police interactions for tourists on motorcycles are generally informal compared to other SE Asian countries. Minor infractions (no helmet) often result in verbal warnings. For more serious situations (no license/docs), local intervention by a Filipino resident often resolves the issue through informal mediation.
  • When riding motorcycles in the Philippines, especially outside major tourist hubs, it is important to be aware of traffic regulations. Police may conduct stops to check for proper licensing and adherence to safety gear requirements, such as wearing a helmet. Operating a motorcycle without the appropriate license can result in fines and confiscation of the vehicle. In case of an accident, especially if another party is involved, not having a valid license can lead to severe legal and financial consequences. While tourist areas might have more relaxed enforcement, it is generally advisable to comply with all traffic laws.
  • Local rum in the Philippines is available for approximately $2.5 per 0.7L. While generally considered to have no hangover effects when consumed moderately, excessive consumption can lead to negative effects. The price in stores is around 57-60 pesos, with a retail price of 75 pesos. It is often consumed with cola by locals when funds are limited.
  • Safety and attractions in Mindanao. An experienced resident dismisses safety concerns as outdated, recommending Mindanao for both living and tourism. Highlights include Samal Island (largest bat colony), Camiguin (multiple attractions), and sites near Davao: Crocodile Park, Enchanted River, Marilog Waterfalls, Pink Mosque, beaches on Samal and Mati, and Mount Apo. Advises against listening to fear-mongers, stating that dangerous areas exist everywhere but can be avoided with common sense. A traveler planning a month-long trip to Davao is seeking architectural sights and hiking/biking opportunities, rather than beach-focused activities.
  • Petty theft (phones, bags) is a risk on public beaches and in hotels in the Philippines. Travelers are advised to keep valuables secured and not to leave belongings unattended in public areas, regardless of the perceived safety of the location.
  • Exporting large seashells or marine specimens from the Philippines is strictly prohibited by customs law. Such souvenirs will be confiscated at the airport airport security control, regardless of purchase receipts.
  • Users are experiencing issues with receiving verification codes via WhatsApp for ride-sharing apps in the Philippines, possibly due to regional settings. While Grab app functions normally, other specialized tricycle apps may not. Users are advised to explore alternative booking methods.
  • In the Philippines, driving with non-standard or 'souvenir' licenses carries risks. While police scrutiny of licenses may vary, and anecdotal evidence suggests some officers may attempt to solicit bribes, official fines for violations typically range from 100-300 PHP and are payable via bank. On Cebu, some travelers report police being reluctant to issue official tickets, preferring on-the-spot cash payments, sometimes negotiated by plainclothes 'helpers'. However, official penalties can include vehicle impoundment. Engaging in bribery is illegal and risks legal repercussions for both parties. It is advisable to possess a valid international driving permit to avoid complications.
  • Winged insects commonly found in tropical environments like the Philippines that shed their wings indoors are typically termites. They are not harmful to humans and do not bite; however, they are wood-destroying organisms.
  • On June 8, 2026, a magnitude 7.8 earthquake struck Mindanao, Philippines. Aftershocks were felt across the region, with some areas experiencing up to 6.9 magnitude. News reports indicated a high probability of a tsunami, leading to evacuation recommendations for coastal residents. Damage reported includes collapsed buildings in some areas. Power outages occurred. Frequent aftershocks, including a 5.1 magnitude tremor, were recorded. The epicenter was located offshore, contributing to tsunami concerns.
  • Operating drones for aerial photography in the Philippines may require permissions. While some users have reported launching drones without issue in areas like Cebu (near Jpark hotel) and Moalboal, it's crucial to verify current regulations. Specific rules can vary by location and may necessitate permits or registration. Authorities may be stricter in certain areas or for commercial use. It's advisable to research and obtain any required permits before flying to avoid potential penalties.
  • Sending a 15-year-old solo to Cebu for language school requires careful consideration of her independence, navigational skills, and the safety of the destination. While some airlines offer unaccompanied minor services, verify age restrictions and procedures. The Philippines can be challenging for first-time solo travelers due to language barriers and potentially complex transportation systems. Researching school-provided airport reception, pre-arranged transport, and familiarizing the student with emergency protocols is crucial. Consider the potential for stress and the student's comfort level with navigating unfamiliar environments independently. Ensure the chosen school environment fosters English use among students from diverse backgrounds.
  • Assessing safety in Mindanao for tourists. Initial inquiries suggest eastern Mindanao might be safe. However, conflicting reports emerge: one user describes it as a 'Filipino Caucasus' with militant groups, kidnappings for ransom targeting Western tourists, and open conflicts. His Filipina wife advises against it for 'white people.' Conversely, long-term residents (5+ years) and frequent travelers dismiss these fears as outdated, stating eastern and northeastern parts are safe with normal precautions. They recommend visiting specific tourist spots like Samal Island (bat colony), Camiguin, Davao Crocodile Park, Enchanted River, Marilog waterfalls, Mount Apo, and a pink mosque. They advise against night travel alone with money, but stress this is standard for the Philippines.
  • Exporting corals from the Philippines is illegal under bioresource protection laws. Penalties range from 20,000 PHP fines to imprisonment. Strictly prohibited in both checked and carry-on luggage.
  • The Makati district in Manila is generally considered safe for evening walks, even at night, due to security measures like gated alleys. While other areas like Pasay might show signs of poverty, overall, Manila is not inherently dangerous for tourists, provided they exercise common sense and avoid displaying wealth conspicuously.
  • For safety in Manila, it is recommended to use taxis for transportation, especially at night, and to avoid walking in unfamiliar areas. On the islands, daytime travel is generally considered safe. Standard travel precautions should be observed in all locations.
  • Diving safety protocols in the Philippines: 1. Always use a personal dive computer; do not rely solely on the guide's timing. 2. AOWD certification is often required for sites with strong currents (e.g., Balicasag). 3. Always perform safety stops as dictated by the computer, regardless of guide instructions. 4. eLearning (PADI) can be completed prior to arrival to save time during the vacation.
  • Smoking regular cigarettes on Bohol, Philippines, can result in a fine of 1000 Philippine pesos if caught. However, it's commonly observed that many people still smoke, and enforcement may not be strict.
  • Perceptions of the Philippines as a travel destination vary significantly. Some find it dilapidated, dirty, and populated by beggars, comparing unfavorably to domestic travel options. Others view it as an affordable alternative to Russia for exploration, despite acknowledging the country's economic challenges and the presence of poverty. There are also mentions of seeking counterfeit documents for perceived convenience.
  • To check real-time tide levels and general weather conditions in coastal areas of the Philippines, utilize 24/7 webcams, which provide live footage. This allows for accurate assessment of water levels, which can fluctuate significantly.
  • Solo female travel is generally safe in the Philippines, particularly in tourist island regions. Caution is advised in Manila, where it is recommended to use official transportation services and stay in reputable neighborhoods. Maintaining a modest appearance can help mitigate unwanted attention.
  • Manila is a dense and chaotic metropolitan area with significant impoverished districts, making it poorly suited for a relaxed trip with young children or elderly travelers. Boracay, Cebu, or destinations in Thailand and Vietnam are recommended as more comfortable and safer alternatives.
