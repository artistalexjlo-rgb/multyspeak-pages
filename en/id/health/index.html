Health in Indonesia: what actually matters to know

Real experience of people from chats on «Health» in Indonesia — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • For severe diarrhea/vomiting in Indonesia: use Nifuroxazide (intestinal antiseptic) available via Grab delivery from local pharmacies. Seek immediate medical attention if dehydration is severe.
  • Be aware of methanol poisoning risks from unregulated alcohol. In case of poisoning, preserve the container for analysis. Medical treatment in Bali for severe toxicity (e.g., blood filtration) is extremely expensive, costing hundreds of millions of IDR. Ensure travel/expatriate insurance coverage is active and specifically covers emergency evacuation.
  • Driving a scooter in Indonesia requires an international driving permit with a valid 'A' (motorcycle) category. Local Indonesian driver licenses for tourists without a KITAS are often illegal or invalid in case of accidents. Driving without the correct license category makes you legally liable for any accident and invalidates insurance coverage.
  • Users discuss Genki travel insurance costs, with plans like 'Genki Explorer' costing around €72/month and another user paying €52.5. Pricing varies based on add-ons and possibly age. Some users express dissatisfaction with Genki's customer support, citing slow response times in emergencies.
  • The most effective and widely available local mosquito repellent in Bali is 'Soffel'. Travelers recommend the cream version over the spray for longer-lasting protection. For treating rooms indoors, 'Baygon' spray is effective but requires subsequent ventilation to keep children and pets safe.
  • Popular pharmaceutical alternatives to western flu powders like TeraFlu in Indonesia include: Tylolhot (hot drink powder), Decolgen (tablets for fever/congestion), Paramex Flu & Batuk, and Bodrex Flu & Batuk. These are widely available at local pharmacies without a prescription.
  • Eye irritation and watering after swimming in the ocean in Bali can be caused by allergies. Antihistamine eye drops or oral antihistamines (like Loratadine) can help alleviate symptoms. If symptoms persist or worsen, consult a medical professional.
  • For short-term stays in Indonesia, contact your insurance provider's assistance service as soon as a health issue arises. They will guide you on the next steps based on your situation. If experiencing severe allergic reactions like hives, contact them immediately.
  • Bite or scratch incidents involving mammals in Bali (monkeys, bats, mice) require immediate medical attention to assess the risk of rabies. Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) including rabies vaccination is standard protocol. Travelers should consult local clinics (Puskesmas or private hospitals) immediately after exposure, regardless of the perceived minor nature of the wound.
  • A user lost an important health-related item on Nusa Penida and is now on Gili Trawangan. They are seeking assistance from anyone traveling between these locations or from Nusa Penida to Bali to retrieve and deliver the item.
  • There are recurring reports of methanol poisoning from adulterated alcohol in Bali. Symptoms include sudden nausea, dizziness, light sensitivity, and severe respiratory distress. Seek immediate hospital care (BIMC or similar) if poisoning is suspected. Avoid unbranded or cheap local alcohol sold in informal settings.
  • Alcohol safety on Bali: To avoid methanol poisoning, purchase alcohol only from reputable sources such as supermarkets, wine shops, and established restaurants. Avoid cheap, unbranded alcohol from street vendors or informal sellers, as it may be counterfeit or contain harmful substances like methanol.
  • High humidity in Bali significantly slows down wound healing. Standard protocol: clean with hydrogen peroxide or chlorhexidine, keep dry, and avoid sea water. Tetanus (Tdap) vaccination is highly recommended after motorcycle accidents involving road rash.
  • Travel insurance is mandatory for medical emergencies in Indonesia, especially for motorbike accidents. Treatment costs for serious injuries (like surgery for finger/hand trauma) can exceed 138,000,000 IDR. Standard tourist insurance often does not cover motorbike operation unless the rider has a valid license.
  • Medical costs in Indonesia, especially at private hospitals like BIMC, can reach 300 million IDR for a few days of intensive care. Travel insurance is highly recommended even for short-term stays due to risks including traffic accidents, food poisoning, and animal bites. Standard policies are available via premium banking subscriptions or dedicated travel insurance aggregators.
  • Driving a scooter in Bali without previous experience is highly dangerous due to traffic conditions. Essential requirements: valid motorcycle driving license (Category A) and international driving permit. Without a license, travel insurance policies typically do not cover accident-related medical expenses, which can be extremely high. It is highly recommended to take professional driving lessons before attempting to ride in traffic.
  • There are reports of suspected methanol poisoning from counterfeit alcohol in Bali, including local wine and arak. While the exact cause and scale are under investigation, it is advised to purchase alcoholic beverages from reputable supermarkets rather than from street vendors or small shops to minimize risk. Overconsumption of methanol can be fatal.
  • Driving a scooter without a valid license (International Driving Permit with Category A) in Indonesia can result in fines up to 1,000,000 IDR and vehicle impoundment. Crucially, most travel insurance policies will deny medical claims for accidents occurring without a valid license.
  • For motorbike riding in Bali, hiring an instructor is highly recommended to avoid injuries. Essential safety gear includes a helmet, valid license, and insurance. The clutch is on the left, and the brake on the right. Standard intersection rules apply.
  • For travel insurance covering surfing in Bali, consider options that include medical coverage for extreme sports. Some banks, like TBank with their premium subscription, offer comprehensive insurance for individuals and families, covering up to 5 people and including business lounge access.
  • Travelers arriving in Indonesia must fill out the 'All Indonesia' electronic arrival card 72 hours before arrival. A physical return ticket (not just a booking) is often required by immigration. Travel insurance is highly recommended even if not strictly enforced at the gate.
  • Coral cuts are prone to infection due to debris remaining in the wound. Immediate disinfection with lime or vinegar is recommended. If inflammation persists, topical antibiotics containing Neomycin Sulfate are commonly used. Over-treatment by local clinics is a recurring concern reported by expats.
  • Availability of methanol test kits in Bali pharmacies: There is a difficulty in finding readily available methanol test kits in local pharmacies in Bali. Despite the need, especially in areas where alcohol-related incidents occur, these kits are not easily accessible. Inquiries to laboratories indicate they do not offer such testing services.
  • Travelers in Bali should be wary of suspicious offers for 'local health insurance for premium hospitals' from unverified individuals, especially if priced unusually low (e.g., $80 for 3 months) or claiming to be a 'European' policy. Such offers are highly likely to be scams. Always purchase health insurance from reputable and recognized insurance providers. For comparison, some banks (e.g., T-Bank in Russia) offer premium travel insurance for multiple individuals with broader coverage.
  • Instances of methanol poisoning in locally-produced or counterfeit alcoholic beverages sold in Bali have been reported. It is advised to avoid purchasing local wine or 'arak' from unverified, small convenience stores. Stick to reputable liquor stores (e.g., Bottle Avenue) or imported brands to minimize risk.
  • Indonesian hospitals, including those in Bali, frequently require upfront cash deposits, local bank transfers, or immediate card payments for emergency admissions and surgeries before starting medical procedures, regardless of the severity of the patient's condition, unless an official Letter of Authorization (LOA) from a verified insurance company is instantly provided. Travelers must maintain access to emergency funds and choose reliable travel insurance with fast direct-billing response times.
  • Comparing costs between Southeast Asian destinations requires a holistic approach, including airfare and visa expenses, not just local item prices. Medicine and specific services can vary significantly; sometimes medical tourism between neighboring countries is cheaper than local retail purchase.
  • For symptoms of food poisoning ('Bali Belly') such as diarrhea, vomiting, and weakness, local pharmacies (Apotek) stock effective medications containing Attapulgite (such as Entrostop). It is a powerful adsorbent; typical dosage is limited to 2 tablets a day to prevent severe constipation.
  • Traffic fines in Indonesia are regulated by Law No. 22 of 2009. Common local traffic fines: Driving without a license: 1,000,000 IDR. Driving without a helmet: 250,000 IDR per person. Driving against traffic/on sidewalks: 500,000 IDR. While 'on-the-spot' payments are common, they are technically informal/corrupt practices. Lack of a proper driver's license (Category A for motorbikes) invalidates most travel insurance policies in the event of an accident.
  • Standard travel and local health insurance policies in Bali (such as Genki) generally do not cover specialized diagnostic tests or treatment for pre-existing/chronic conditions unless there is an acute emergency. Additionally, new insurance policies typically have a waiting period of 5 to 7 days before coverage begins. For comprehensive or non-emergency specialized check-ups, it is recommended to get tested in your home country due to high medical costs and strict approval rules on the island.
  • High UV exposure near the equator can cause significant sunburns in as little as 30 minutes during peak hours (11:00–16:00). Daily use of SPF is essential for skin safety, and outdoor activities are recommended before 10:00 or after 16:00.
  • Qdenga is a dengue vaccine approved for individuals aged 6 to 45. It is a two-dose series administered with a three-month interval. It is available at facilities like Siloam Hospital on Bali. Unlike older vaccines, it is effective for those who have never had dengue before. Always consult a physician before vaccination.
  • A skin reaction after snorkeling in Gili Air, characterized by redness, itching, dryness, and a rough texture, is likely a mild sting from jellyfish or plankton, which is common in the area. Recommended treatments include oral antihistamines (e.g., Zyrtec,.); topical hydrocortisone cream or Fenistil gel to reduce itching and swelling. Avoid scratching and rinsing with fresh water if the sting is recent. Seek medical attention if symptoms worsen or the rash spreads within 2 days.
  • When traveling from mainland Bali to Nusa Penida, departing from Kusamba Port in East Bali is a highly recommended alternative to Sanur Port. It shortens the open water speedboat crossing to just 20 minutes (as opposed to 40-50 minutes from Sanur), minimizing motion sickness from rough sea waves. Rental motorbikes are widely available for hire directly at the Kusamba pier upon arrival.
  • For a sore throat or early cold symptoms in Indonesia, recommend 'Tolak Angin'—a widely available traditional herbal syrup. It can be mixed into hot tea as a natural sugar substitute to soothe throat irritation.
  • For travel insurance in Bali, consider T-Bank's premium subscription which covers up to 5 people and includes business lounge access. Ensure the policy covers activities like surfing if needed. If experiencing a health issue, contact your insurance provider's assistance service immediately.
  • Skin irritation after contact with corals or reef structures in Bali can be caused by microorganisms living on them, resulting in a burn-like sensation. It's advisable to consult a doctor or use over-the-counter remedies like Banéočin if available, and avoid scratching or wetting the affected area.
  • Medical emergencies on Bali can be managed by contacting local ambulance services via WhatsApp. Note: Local ambulances often function as medical transport rather than mobile intensive care units. Keep a list of verified ambulance WhatsApp contacts saved for urgent situations.
  • Tap water in Bali is generally not potable. Even for brushing teeth, bottled or filtered water is strongly recommended for visitors to avoid gastrointestinal issues, as water storage tanks can be contaminated.
  • For visitors recovering from food poisoning (Bali Belly) who need bland, safe food, recommend 'Hainan Chicken Rice' (Nasi Ayam Hainan) from Chinese or local restaurants. It consists of plain boiled rice, steamed chicken breast, and clear broth, which is very easy on a sensitive stomach.
  • Tap water on Bali is not potable. While locals may use it for brushing teeth, tourists are highly advised to use bottled or filtered water to avoid gastrointestinal issues. Ice in cafes is generally safe if it is uniform, factory-made ice (cylindrical shape with a hole), but avoid ice in unverified street-side food stalls.
  • For travel to Bali, T-Bank (formerly Tinkoff) Premium subscription offers insurance coverage. Key points: it is valid for up to 90 days of stay, but users must leave the country and return (or visit another country) to reset the period. Coverage amount is typically up to 100,000 USD (verify with bank support). The subscription also provides lounge access.
  • In Indonesia, hospitals often withhold treatment until receiving a guarantee of payment letter from international travel insurance. Patients should actively mediate between the insurer's support team and the hospital's billing department to expedite the process during medical emergencies.
  • In case of an animal bite in Indonesia, immediate medical attention at a clinic is mandatory regardless of whether the animal appears domestic. Rabies is a serious threat on the island; post-exposure prophylaxis (vaccine) is the standard recommended medical procedure.
