Transport in Indonesia: what actually matters to know

Real experience of people from chats on «Transport» in Indonesia — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • Toll roads in Bali do not accept cash. Payment is exclusively via a 'toll card' (e.g., e-Toll, e-Money). These cards can be purchased directly from attendants at the toll booths, typically for 100,000 IDR, which includes an initial balance. Contactless payments like Apple Pay (with foreign cards) or local QRIS systems may also be supported, but a physical toll card is the most reliable method.
  • Exchange rates at Bali airport are generally worse than in the city. However, the difference might not be critical for small amounts needed for a SIM card or taxi. For larger sums, it's advisable to exchange money in the city during business days.
  • Passport validity must be at least 6 months at the time of entry into Indonesia. For international transit, especially with separate tickets, travelers must ensure the airline's policy allows boarding, as airlines are strictly liable for transporting passengers with insufficient passport validity for their final destination.
  • Electronic traffic law enforcement (ETLE) has been implemented in Ubud, targeting illegal street parking (specifically on Jalan Raya Ubud). Violators will be fined digitally. Renters of cars or motorbikes should use designated parking spaces to avoid fines that will be forwarded to rental owners and charged to the renter's deposit.
  • Driving without a valid license in Indonesia risks confiscation of the vehicle and official fines. During police raids, negotiating with officers is often ineffective and can lead to vehicle impoundment. Always wear a helmet and dress modestly to attract less attention from traffic authorities.
  • Driving a scooter in Indonesia requires an international driving permit with a valid 'A' (motorcycle) category. Local Indonesian driver licenses for tourists without a KITAS are often illegal or invalid in case of accidents. Driving without the correct license category makes you legally liable for any accident and invalidates insurance coverage.
  • Bali is not a walkable destination. Transportation relies heavily on scooters/bikes and taxis (Grab/Gojek). Travel times are significantly longer than distances suggest due to heavy traffic congestion, particularly in tourist hubs like Canggu. Expect 1.5+ hours for relatively short distances during peak hours.
  • To find a motorbike instructor in the Badung area of Bali, look for local driving schools or individual instructors. Many local 'teachers' offer affordable lessons, often in English. It's recommended to inquire through local community groups or search online for 'motorbike driving lessons Bali' or 'scooter lessons Badung' to find contacts. Some private individuals also offer instruction.
  • An onward or return ticket is a legal requirement for entry into Indonesia. While some travelers successfully bypass checks via automated e-gates, airlines frequently deny boarding at departure points without proof of onward travel. Furthermore, immigration officers regularly conduct manual checks, especially for families. Travelers should possess a valid onward flight ticket or a verifiable booking to avoid entry denial.
  • It may be possible to use electric scooters (e-scooters) in Bali even without a Category A license, but regulations can vary. It's advisable to check local rules and rental company policies.
  • Police stops on motorbikes in Bali are less likely if you are wearing a helmet, dressed appropriately, and following traffic rules. If you ride recklessly (e.g., without a shirt, running red lights), the chance of being stopped increases significantly. There are specific areas where police frequently set up checkpoints.
  • Users are discussing the cost and necessity of repairing minor damages, such as scratches, on rented scooters in Bali. There's a debate on whether these are considered normal wear and tear or require payment.
  • Lake Yeh Malet in Karangasem has officially reopened to visitors after a major clean-up operation that cleared decades of silt and overgrowth. It is safe for swimming and local fishing, and offers clear, scenic views of Mount Agung. It serves as an excellent alternative stopover destination for travelers heading to or from Amed.
  • There is no traditional taxi service on Nusa Penida. The primary means of transportation are rental scooters or hiring a private driver for a daily rate (typically 800k–1,000k IDR for a ~10-hour duration).
  • Speedboat operators at Padang Bai harbor (Bali) and Lombok routinely inflate ticket prices for walk-ins. Always verify pricing on official operator websites or 12go.asia beforehand; presenting these online rates to ticket agents instantly lowers the price (e.g., from 1,000,000 IDR to 600,000 IDR for two people). Additionally, ride-hailing apps like Gojek and Grab do not operate on Nusa Penida. Local port drivers charge high flat rates (minimum 100,000 IDR for short trips), making pre-arranged scooter rentals delivered to the harbor the most cost-effective option.
  • Renters of scooters in Bali must have an International Driving Permit (IDP) with a Class A (motorcycle) endorsement. Almost all rental scooters on the island (like Vario 125cc or NMax 155cc) exceed the engine capacity limits of Class M (moped) or Class B (car) licenses, making them legally invalid.
  • As of June 10, 2026, fuel prices in Indonesia have been adjusted. Pertamax price increased by approximately 30% to 16,250 IDR per liter. Subsidized Pertalite fuel remains stable at 10,000 IDR per liter. Local authorities describe this as a regular cost adjustment.
  • Immigration authorities in Indonesia may conduct 'administrative checks' at the airport during departure. Even with valid visas (e.g., 33g), previous visa history or sponsor company issues can trigger entry/exit restrictions. It is advised to verify visa status with professional agents before travel and ensure all past visa closures were handled correctly.
  • NMAX scooter for rent, model year 2025, with 4 months of usage. Available for long-term rental at 1.6 million per month. Contact via private message.
  • During the peak dry season in August and September, Bali experiences cooler temperatures and cold evening winds driven by winter air masses moving from Australia. Ocean water temperatures also decrease by a few degrees. Travelers planning night-time scooter rides or staying near coastal areas should bring light jackets or hoodies.
  • To find affordable bike rentals in Bali, search 'bike rent' on Google Maps. Many listings include WhatsApp numbers or websites, allowing you to find options like new Yamaha bikes for approximately 3 million IDR per month without requiring a deposit.
  • New traffic management scheme for Uluwatu (Ungasan/Pecatu) starts June 1, 2026, to address congestion near Nirmala intersection. Expect changes in traffic routing at major junctions.
  • When traveling to Nusa Penida from Padangbai, taking a rented scooter on the ferry requires the original vehicle registration document (STNK) and a valid license, despite rental agencies claiming copies are sufficient. Ferries frequently suffer delays of 3 hours or more. The recommended alternative is to take a speedboat as a passenger and rent a scooter on Nusa Penida for approximately 100k - 200k IDR per day.
  • Travelers visiting Gili or Nusa Penida are advised to store large luggage at their Bali hotel or at dedicated 'Self Storage' facilities (e.g., in Padang Bai). Carrying large suitcases onto boats or islands is difficult due to logistics and terrain. Storage facilities in Padang Bai are typically located within 500-700 meters of the ferry/boat departure points.
  • Speed boat services from Bali (Serangan) to Java (Banyuwangi) are operated by Express Bahari, with four departures per week. However, online schedules and booking options may not be available directly on their website. It is recommended to check for available routes and ticketing information.
  • Standard taxi prices from Bali airport are significantly inflated; utilizing ride-sharing apps like Grab or Gojek is highly recommended to avoid overcharging (typical fair price is around 100k-160k IDR). Official airport taxi counters are another safer alternative to avoid extortion.
  • Driving a scooter in Bali without previous experience is highly dangerous due to traffic conditions. Essential requirements: valid motorcycle driving license (Category A) and international driving permit. Without a license, travel insurance policies typically do not cover accident-related medical expenses, which can be extremely high. It is highly recommended to take professional driving lessons before attempting to ride in traffic.
  • Travelers report frequent scams involving fraudulent third-party ticket agents for Bali-to-Island transfers. Verify ticketing agencies through official sources or purchase directly at port ticket counters to avoid 'no-show' scams where bookings are invalid or non-existent.
  • Driving in Bali presents challenges due to narrow streets, traffic congestion, numerous scooters, left-hand traffic, and right-hand side steering wheels. While scooters are common, cars can offer a more relaxed driving experience.
  • For ferry travel from Padang Bai to the Gili Islands, choose large and stable ferries like Eka Jaya, avoiding wooden boats. Ferries departing from Sanur or Serangan might be a longer but cheaper and more convenient option if arriving by taxi.
  • Road reconstruction of the key 8km section Sampalan-Toyapakeh on Nusa Penida started in mid-2026 and is scheduled to last for 240 days. Expect heavy traffic delays and poor road conditions on the western side of the island.
  • Yes, you can buy boat tickets for the Bali-Gili route at the port. However, tickets can sometimes sell out, so it's recommended to purchase them in advance.
  • Driving a scooter without a valid license (International Driving Permit with Category A) in Indonesia can result in fines up to 1,000,000 IDR and vehicle impoundment. Crucially, most travel insurance policies will deny medical claims for accidents occurring without a valid license.
  • To legally drive a motorbike in Bali, you need a valid driver's license. While rentals may not always check for a license, you can obtain an International Driving Permit (IDP) or a local Balinese license. Russian domestic licenses are generally not sufficient for driving legally or for insurance purposes in case of an accident.
  • Riding a motorbike without a helmet is highly dangerous and common in Bali. Local law enforcement and residents strictly enforce helmet rules on toll roads. Wearing a personal, well-fitting helmet is strongly advised.
  • A method for dealing with motorbike stops when riding without a license: hide keys, license, and passport in the hotel. Threaten with towing to negotiate a lower fine.
  • To obtain an Indonesian driving license (SIM) independently, foreigners must visit Polresta Denpasar with a valid KITAS, medical certificates, and pass theoretical and practical driving tests. The actual cost of self-registration runs close to 1.5 million IDR. Because of this, many choose to use local expediting helpers at the station who charge approximately 1.6 million IDR to bypass the testing process entirely.
  • Bike rentals are available at Nusa Penida ports for approximately 150,000 IDR per day. Due to poor road conditions (potholes, loose gravel), renting lighter bikes is recommended over heavy models like NMAX or XMAX for better maneuverability.
  • Officially obtaining a motorcycle license in Indonesia requires a KITAS. 'Buyable' licenses often lack true legal standing in the government database, which can lead to insurance companies denying medical claims after an accident.
  • Renting a scooter without an International Driving Permit (IDP) in Indonesia is common but dangerous. Without a valid license, insurance claims are invalid, and the rider is typically held liable in any accident. Police checks may result in fines or demands for informal payments, though official traffic operations make bribes impossible.
  • To hire a driver for a day in Bali, look for services labeled 'driver for the day'. These can often be found on platforms like Trip.com. The driver will be able to take you to desired locations.

It is advised to have a printed onward ticket to the final destination and to verify visa requirements with the airline during check-in at the departure airport.
