Banking & Money in Bulgaria: real experience from chats

Real experience of people from chats on «Banking & Money» in Bulgaria — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • To open a bank account in Bulgaria in 2024, individuals were required to provide: original and copy of their Russian Tax Identification Number (INN), extract from the Pension Fund, extract from the real estate registry in Russia, both Russian passports and copies, a notarized deed for Bulgarian real estate, a Bulgarian phone number, and a fee of 100 BGN for the application. The application review process took 10 days, followed by 7 days for card issuance.
  • Opening a bank account at Tokuda Bank (Bulgaria) for Visa D: Required documents include a passport and INN (tax ID). The estimated opening fee is approximately 200 EUR. The bank issues payment cards to account holders.
  • When acting as a financial sponsor for a visa application, the primary documentation required pertains to your financial capacity. A work reference letter for the applicant, while potentially useful for their own application, is generally not a substitute for proof of your financial support.
  • Opening a bank account in Bulgaria for residency purposes requires attention to specific bank policies and migration acceptance. While some banks may offer accounts for non-residents, BGN 5 is often cited as a possible opening fee. Crucially, statements from the chosen bank must be accepted by the Bulgarian migration authorities. In Varna, only Bulgarian bank statements are reportedly accepted. Researching banks that meet both transaction needs (deposits, withdrawals, transfers) and migration requirements is essential.
  • Exchanging large denominations like 500 euro bills in Bulgaria can be challenging due to a lack of change. A practical approach is to request a partial exchange (e.g., exchange 200 EUR from a 500 EUR bill) at an exchange office, or to purchase US dollars, which might be more readily accepted or easier to exchange.
  • To exchange or break down 500 Euro banknotes in Bulgaria, visit a bank where you hold an account (they have counterfeit detection equipment) or try large casinos which typically handle high-value cash transactions.
  • Russian citizens may face difficulties opening bank accounts in Bulgaria, but some banks, such as BACB, have been reported to still open accounts. For property sales by Russian citizens in Bulgaria, several methods exist for fund transfer: 1. **Lawyer's Account Transfer**: Funds can be transferred from the buyer's Bulgarian account to a lawyer's account, and then from the lawyer's account to the seller's Russian bank account (e.g., Raiffeisen Bank in Russia). This service is often facilitated by realtors. 2. **Rubles in Russia, Leva/Euro in Bulgaria**: A "counter-flow" scheme where a Russian buyer pays rubles to the Russian seller in Russia, while a Bulgarian buyer pays leva/euro to the Bulgarian seller in Bulgaria, effectively offsetting the transaction. 3. **SWIFT Transfers**: SWIFT remains an option, but may involve significant exchange rate losses and potential compliance hurdles. 4. **Cash Export**: Another method, though subject to currency export regulations and risks.
  • For BACB (Bank of Athens Bulgaria): Opening an account can take within a day. Obtaining a card costs extra and takes 3 days, while a free card takes a week. The fee for account review was previously 100 BGN.
  • Russian banking apps (like Sberbank) are accessible from Bulgaria, often without a VPN, though connectivity can be intermittent. If an app fails to connect, using a VPN is a standard solution. Some users suggest secondary banking apps like DomRF as a stable alternative for managing Russian accounts from abroad.
  • Bulgarian Schengen visa processing for Russian citizens is currently experiencing significant delays (up to 2 months). SMS notifications from visa centers are unreliable; users should track status via the official portal using the receipt's QR code. Visas are often issued with dates post-dated relative to original flight bookings, forcing users to reschedule. There is no special 'property owner' visa category; all are Tourist C visas. Financial proof (50 EUR/day) can be demonstrated via bank statements, including credit card limits, though funds must be sufficient and liquid.
  • Opening a bank account in Bulgaria (e.g., BACB in Burgas) may require a passport (foreign and domestic), a tax identification number (INN) on a form, and an employment contract. Expect a fee of around 100 EUR for account opening, with cards ready in about a week. A Bulgarian phone number is recommended for SMS notifications. Real estate ownership is generally not a requirement.
  • For large currency exchanges in Bulgaria when banks are closed, look for exchange offices on Alexandrovska Street (Burgas). Always explicitly request new-series banknotes (clean, no stains, with a blue security strip) before the transaction, as older notes may be difficult to exchange later.
  • Non-Russian bank cards generally work in Bulgaria. MasterCard and cards from countries like Kyrgyzstan and Kazakhstan are functional. Bulgarian issued cards also work seamlessly. While cash withdrawals are possible, be mindful of potential significant bank fees. The key is that the card is not Russian.
  • In Bulgaria, acceptance of large Euro banknotes (e.g., €200, €500) in retail stores like Kaufland can be problematic due to insufficient change or store policies. * **€200 notes:** May be accepted, but expect delays as cashiers might struggle to find change, especially for smaller purchases. * **€500 notes:** Often not accepted directly in stores; they might only be manageable through bank deposits. * **Official policy vs. practice:** While some establishments (e.g., Varna Aquarium) may state an obligation to accept large notes and provide change, practical implementation varies. * **Advice:** It is recommended to carry smaller denominations of Euro or local currency (BGN) for daily purchases to avoid inconvenience.
  • The functionality of Belarusian bank cards in Bulgaria is questioned, with no definitive confirmation of their widespread acceptance or specific locations where they might still operate.
  • Individuals participating in Bulgaria's 'wealthy pensioner' program can open a bank account in Bulgaria even while holding a Russian passport and being on a tourist visa. The Bulgarian-American Credit Bank (BACB) is specifically mentioned as an institution where this process is feasible.
  • Large Euro banknotes (500 EUR) are often difficult to exchange or use at standard retail registers in Bulgaria. Successful usage reported at Kaufland self-checkout kiosks, which automatically verify the note's authenticity. Users advise against using them for very small purchases to avoid suspicion or rejection by staff.
  • When depositing cash into Bulgarian bank accounts, amounts over 5,000 EUR may trigger a request for a declaration of origin. Depositing via ATM may be subject to different rules than branch counters. If large sums are needed, depositing via a family member's account can sometimes avoid individual limit issues. Commissions for cash deposits (vnoska) vary by bank (e.g., DSK, BACB, Tokuda).
  • To resolve MTS roaming issues in Bulgaria: 1. Manually select a local network in phone settings. 2. Switch network preference from LTE to 2G/3G, as LTE may cause registration errors. 3. Ensure roaming is enabled via the carrier's support or personal account. 4. Use VoWiFi (Voice over Wi-Fi) as a fallback for receiving bank SMS and making calls without needing cellular roaming.
  • UnionPay cards have limited acceptance in Bulgaria. They may work in specific ATMs (e.g., ATB banks) but are not universally accepted for payments in all shops, restaurants, or payment terminals. It is advisable for travelers to carry alternative payment methods or sufficient cash.
  • For a Bulgarian Visa D application, bank statements are crucial. A certificate from the bank is typically valid for only one month. If your application is in November and your visa validity is until August, a bank statement from August will likely not be accepted. It is strongly recommended to obtain a fresh bank statement no more than a month before your visa application submission date. Relying on a printout from a mobile app alongside an old certificate might not be sufficient. It's best to obtain an official bank statement with a wet stamp or electronic signature close to your application date.
  • Users with Bulgarian CCB (Visa) cards may experience authentication errors when trying to make payments on Booking.com. This issue is suspected to be related to CCB's lack of SMS verification for transactions, which the bank has reportedly refused to implement. Be prepared for potential payment difficulties with this card for online bookings.
  • Russian citizens may face account freezes in Bulgarian banks after receiving funds from real estate sales. This is a known issue and potential preceent that users should be aware of.
  • For non-residents in Bulgaria, BACB (Bulgarian-American Credit Bank) and Tokuda Bank are the most commonly cited options for opening accounts. Requirements vary by branch; if a specific location demands proof of employment, try a different branch. Tokuda Bank has limited functionality (no SWIFT, restricted card management).
  • UnionPay cards, including those from Asian-Pacific Bank, generally work in Bulgaria but with significant limitations and poor reliability, especially for cash withdrawals. It is not recommended as a primary or reliable method of payment/cash access.
  • Bulgaria has fully transitioned to the Euro. The Bulgarian Lev (BGN) is no longer legal tender. Remaining BGN currency can only be exchanged at financial institutions; it is not accepted for retail transactions.
  • Bank statements submitted for visa applications are typically valid for one month. It is crucial to obtain the statement shortly before your visa submission date to ensure it is current and meets the consulate's requirements. Relying on outdated statements or unofficial printouts may lead to rejection.
  • When in Bulgaria, it's generally best to use Bulgarian Lev (BGN). While Euros are widely accepted in tourist areas, the exchange rate might not be favorable, and older denominations might be refused by private individuals or in certain less-touristy establishments. For example, one user reported a refusal of a 500 Euro note and inconsistent acceptance of cards at Kaufland supermarkets. It's advisable to have local currency or newer Euro banknotes for transactions.
  • Non-residents with accounts in Bulgarian banks may need to undergo an annual account review. This typically requires an in-person visit to the bank with a passport, national ID card, and potentially a property deed (notary act). Remote updates are generally not possible. Individuals without residency (VGN/PMJ) might be exempt from these requirements.
  • All series of Euro banknotes, including those issued in 2002, are legal tender in Bulgaria and are accepted in shops and for transactions. There is no distinction between "old" and "new" series of Euro banknotes for their validity.
