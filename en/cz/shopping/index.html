Shopping: how it works in Czechia — first-hand

Real experience of people from chats on «Shopping» in Czechia — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • High-quality, small black cumin (Zira) might be difficult to find in Prague. It was found in a packaged product at an 'Albert' store. General availability of this spice in Prague may be limited to larger green varieties.
  • For unique Prague souvenirs beyond typical items: 1. **Beer**: Large bottles of specific local brews, e.g., 'U Fleku' (1-2 litres). 2. **Food**: Irish cheddar cheese (e.g., black label Tesco), local specialty sausages (like fuet), traditional Czech cakes like 'Marlenka' or 'Medovnik'. 'Marlenka' uses more preservatives for shelf stability, 'Medovnik' is traditionally less preserved and requires refrigeration. 3. **Alcohol**: Czech Ice Wine (Ledové víno): Made from grapes harvested at -7°C or lower, remaining frozen during harvest and processing, with must sugar content of at least 27 degrees normalized must weight. A small bottle can be expensive, recommend keeping the receipt for gifting context. 4. **Cultural items**: Items related to Franz Kafka (e.g., coffee cups, portraits, books) available at the Franz Kafka Museum (Cihelná 635, 118 00 Malá Strana, Prague).
  • Promotional information for VTB credit card: Up to 200 days with 0% interest for balance transfers, up to 110 days with 0% interest for purchases. Free issuance, service, and delivery. No commission for cash withdrawals and transfers. Up to 50,000 within the first 30 days. Cashback up to 15% in rubles. Category selection monthly. Decision in 5 minutes. Special offers for new users.
  • MAKRO is recommended for purchasing fresh fish in the Czech Republic. It offers high-quality fresh seafood, though it does not typically sell live fish.
  • In the Czech Republic, individuals can utilize cooperative distilleries (pěstitelské pálenice) to process surplus fruit into spirits. This is a legalized and regulated practice where citizens provide raw materials for distillation services.
  • Cumin in the Czech Republic is called 'římský kmín'. While standard supermarkets like Albert, Billa, or Kaufland rarely stock it, larger Tesco stores sometimes do. For authentic cumin and Central Asian ingredients (like plov rice), visit 'Hala 22' at the Holešovice Market (Holešovická tržnice) in Prague, where an Uzbek merchant sells spices. Note that Hala 22 is closed on Sundays.
  • Risk of proxy fraud: Individuals soliciting help to receive packages or handle gifts in a foreign city are often recruiting 'drops' for illegal activities. The items sent are frequently purchased with stolen credit cards. Accepting these packages can lead to legal prosecution for money laundering or receipt of stolen goods.
  • Ice wine (Ledové víno) can be found in various wine shops in Prague. Recommended locations include Vinotéka Karlák (Karlovo namesti 17), Přítlucká wine shop chain, Vinotéka Kavárna (Korunní 1075/103), Best Drink (Náchodská 2548/77), and Vinotéka z radosti (Bělohorská 128). It is less commonly found in duty-free shops.
  • In Prague, souvenir prices are generally consistent across the city, with notable exceptions of higher prices in tourist-heavy areas like Old Town Square (Staroměstské náměstí), Wenceslas Square (Václavské náměstí), and markets situated between them. To find better prices, explore shops located away from these central tourist hubs.
  • For souvenir shopping in Prague, consider the following: For typical souvenirs like magnets and postcards, check stores like LUXOR, which have locations on Wenceslas Square and in Palladium mall. For potentially more unique items and possibly better prices, explore markets like 'Tamda Foods' within the SAPA market area. Specific prices may vary.
  • For Kyrgyz citizens applying for a long-term student visa to the Czech Republic (e.g., for a Foundation Year program), the application process for a long-term visa often requires travel to the Czech Embassy in Astana, Kazakhstan. Financial proof for a student visa requires showing approximately 116,000 CZK (around 5,000 EUR) per person for one year of stay. For two individuals, this amounts to roughly 10,000 EUR for a year. While some estimates for daily expenses (accommodation and food) can be high (e.g., 100 EUR/day for two in a pension), cooking at home can significantly reduce food costs to around 500-600 EUR per month for two people. It is crucial to prepare thoroughly for the visa interview, particularly for applicants over 20, by reviewing embassy feedback (e.g., on 2GIS) and memorizing all details about the study program.
  • For budget-friendly sports viewing in Prague, Hotel Chodov is suggested as an affordable option. Approximate prices: beer ~49 CZK, meals ~240 CZK. When providing venue recommendations, clarify that 'budget' is subjective and prices can fluctuate.
  • To find fresh herbs in Prague, consider the following locations: Anděl market (Tuesdays and Fridays), Naplavka market (Saturdays), or the 22nd pavilion at Holešovicka tržnice. Large grocery stores may also have options.
