Opening a bank account: how it works in Brazil — first-hand

Real experience of people from chats on «Opening a bank account» in Brazil — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • Opening a bank account in Brazil usually requires a Passport, CPF (Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas), and proof of residence (rental agreement). Some institutions may require an RNM (Registro Nacional Migratório). Requirements vary significantly by bank branch; digital banks like Nubank are often cited as more accessible alternatives.
  • Opening a bank account in Brazil with just a temporary RNM protocol (rather than the physical plastic card) is highly restricted. Neobanks like Nubank and Revolut strictly require the physical RNM card. Traditional banks like Itaú often reject applications automatically at the system level despite manual manager initiation. Banco do Brasil (BB) remains the most reliable option for protocol holders, though card issuance takes about 2 weeks.
  • Opening a bank account in Brazil as a tourist on a visa-free stay or tourist visa is extremely difficult. Most local banks require residency status (VNZ/RNM) to process account applications.
  • Opening bank accounts in Brazil for foreigners: Generally requires Brazilian identification documents like a CPF or a residency permit (Visto de Permanência/protocolo). Some banks may require additional proof of address and employment. While challenging, options might include specific bank branches (e.g., Banco do Brasil with sufficient documentation), or potentially using services linked to cryptocurrency cards.
  • Opening a traditional Brazilian bank account typically requires a permanent residency permit (VNJ) in addition to CPF. While some digital neo-banks or crypto card services might theoretically allow account opening with just CPF, success rates have decreased recently, and additional documentation may still be requested. For most foreigners, VNJ is a prerequisite for a standard bank account.
  • Opening a bank account with a debit card as a tourist with only a CPF in Brazil, particularly at Banco do Brasil, is highly improbable. Reports indicate rejections due to the banking system requiring a local document beyond the CPF, making it challenging for non-residents.
  • CPF (Brazilian taxpayer registry) can be applied for online by filling out the Receita Federal form and sending documents via email. However, having a CPF is currently insufficient for tourists to open a local bank account, as Brazilian banks generally restrict account creation for non-residents.
  • Acceptable documents for address verification when opening a bank account in Brazil include: TIM mobile phone bill, Airbnb booking confirmation, or a valid rental agreement (e.g., 'quinto andar' contract, even if initiated some time ago but still active). It's advisable to provide multiple documents, with the TIM bill being a likely accepted option.
  • Foreigners without Brazilian residency (VNJ) may struggle to open a bank account. An Argentinian DNI is generally not sufficient. However, some individuals have successfully opened accounts at Banco do Brasil by providing proof of residence and explaining their long-term stay and ongoing residency permit application (protocolo de VNJ).
  • Foreigners in Brazil can open bank accounts with Bradesco and Banco do Brasil. These banks support PIX. Transfers from cryptocurrency exchanges like Bybit to local cards (reais) are possible through these banks. Specific protocols might be required for account opening.
  • It is possible for tourists to open a bank account (e.g., at Banco do Brasil in São Paulo) in Brazil. Required documents typically include a Russian passport, a valid tourist visa, CPF, proof of address, and a Brazilian mobile number. A Visa card will be mailed to the provided address within approximately 10 days.
  • In Brazil, the Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas (CPF) registration is not strictly bound to a specific residential address. Foreigners and tourists can register a CPF physically at local offices (Receita Federal) and are free to use a different address when applying to open local bank accounts along their travel route.
  • Opening bank accounts and obtaining credit/debit cards in Brazil as a non-resident can be challenging. Many banks require a Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas (CPF) and proof of residency, or an existing Visto Temporário (temporary visa). Some banks, like Nubank, may have specific requirements or deny applications based on credit history or lack of local ID. Options for non-residents may include: 1. **Sicredi:** Reports suggest it might open accounts with just a CPF. 2. **Santander:** Some individuals have successfully opened accounts. 3. **Bradesco:** May offer options, but specific requirements need verification. 4. **Banco Rendimento:** Website navigation can be difficult; CPF might be a prerequisite. For international usability, it's important to check if the card supports international transactions and if the bank has specific protocols for travel notifications. SMS verification is often linked to a local phone number, which can be an issue with roaming.
  • Rental requirements in Brazil include a valid CPF, proof of income (often from a Brazilian bank account), and frequently a large security deposit (3-12 months). Platforms like Kinto Andar may require proof of recurring income or significant savings held in a local account. Administrative bureaucracy often creates a 'circular' requirement cycle: you need a rental contract to open a bank account, but sometimes you need a bank account to prove financial stability for the rental.
  • Opening bank accounts in Brazil as a tourist with a Russian passport is possible, though it may require specific banks and documentation. Banks like 'green,' 'red,' and 'blue' (often fintech or online-focused) may allow account opening with a CPF, phone number, and address, sometimes remotely. Travelers might need to bypass status checks or try multiple times. Some banks might not have apps in international app stores. Be aware that investment products may have stricter requirements than basic accounts or credit cards.
  • Opening a bank account in Brazil without a permanent residency (RNM) can be challenging but is possible with certain banks like C6 or PicPay. These banks may require an active CPF (Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas) and may grant accounts to non-residents. Nubank is another popular option, but its requirements can change. Increased activity and usage of your CPF can improve your chances of account approval. Be aware that banking regulations and bank policies in Brazil can change frequently, so it is advisable to check the latest requirements directly with the banks.
  • Opening a traditional bank account in Brazil requires residency documents (RNE/RNM). Non-residents can try digital banking alternatives like NG Cash, which may accept CPF and foreign passports, though it typically involves a monthly fee.
  • Opening a bank account in Brazil without residency is extremely difficult. Online banks like Wise and Revolut require a physical RNM (residence card) and will not open accounts with just a CPF. Non-residents/tourists can try mobile wallets like RecargaPay or InfinitePay, though approval is inconsistent. For those with a VNJ Protocol (temporary paper slip before the physical RNM card), major banks like Caixa, Bradesco, Nubank, and C6 usually refuse service. The only bank with a realistic chance of accepting a paper protocol is Banco do Brasil, though you may need to try multiple branches.
  • To open a bank account at Banco do Brasil as a foreigner, you need a passport, CPF, and proof of address. Portuguese language skills are highly recommended. If the clerk refuses, politely ask to speak with the manager ('chefe') to secure an account approval, though initial credit limits may be low.
  • Opening a virtual bank card in Brazil: Requirements generally include Brazilian identification documents. CPF and a local phone number alone are usually insufficient for opening accounts with most banks, including Nubank. Payment via Pix might be possible without CPF for some transactions, but registration on platforms like Bybit may still require it.
  • To open a bank account in Brazil, one typically needs a residency permit (VNJ). With the VNJ protocol and proof of address, one can visit traditional banks like Banco do Brasil. Neobanks like NuBank may offer a simpler process. Banco do Brasil is often considered the main Brazilian state-owned bank. Wise is recommended for international transfers. Crédito consignado (deducted from salary) is a type of loan often associated with government employees but can be offered by various banks.
  • Opening a traditional Brazilian bank account (Banco do Brasil, Itaú, Caixa) as a tourist is extremely difficult as they typically require permanent residency (RNE/RNM) and proof of address. Tourists needing local payment solutions (PIX) are advised to look into crypto-platforms that support PIX, such as ByBit or digital wallets like WanderWallet, as traditional neo-banks (Nubank, C6) also mandate local plastic ID cards.
  • Opening a Brazilian bank account without permanent residency (RNE/CRNM) is difficult. Traditional banks (Banco do Brasil, Itaú) are highly restrictive for tourists. Fintech options like Nubank or PicPay are more accessible, though some (C6 Bank) charge for account maintenance and debit card issuance. International transfers via SWIFT to Brazilian accounts are often slow, expensive (due to exchange rate spreads and bank fees), and prone to compliance blocks. Alternative methods like P2P or regulated crypto-to-fiat gateways are often preferred for higher volumes.
  • Opening a bank account in Brazil without a Brazilian ID (RNE/CRNM) or other formal residency status is difficult and often not possible for tourists. It is recommended to check the official bank websites for specific requirements. Carrying large amounts of cash can be inconvenient due to fluctuating exchange rates. International cards are generally more convenient, but obtaining a local card without residency is challenging.
  • CPF (Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas) is a mandatory 11-digit taxpayer ID in Brazil. It is essential for opening bank accounts, buying property, and accessing government services. It can be obtained at the Federal Police (Polícia Federal) or via gov.br. While technically an ID number, it is treated as a primary identity credential for almost all administrative procedures in the country.
  • Banco Rendimento allows non-residents to open bank accounts in Brazil. Requirements include proof of address outside of Brazil. Expect additional verification questions from bank staff.
  • Foreigners in Brazil may face issues opening bank accounts due to automated system restrictions on document formats (e.g., missing middle names). Some branches can override this via manual verification by contacting the central office. The process takes approximately 1.5 hours.
  • Foreigners holding only a provisional refugee protocol (protocolo de solicitação de refúgio) can open physical bank accounts in Brazil. Banco do Brasil is the most reliable institution for this. To open the account, the applicant must present the valid paper protocol and a proof of address (comprovante de residência). Digital banks like Nubank or traditional ones like Santander generally require the physical RNM/RNE card and will reject the paper protocol.
  • Opening a bank account in Brazil as a foreigner, even without a permanent resident visa (Visto Permanente), is often possible. Visiting a bank branch with funds to deposit or stating that you are expecting a transfer can facilitate the process. Banks like Bradesco may be open to opening accounts under these circumstances. While some banks offer rapid online applications (e.g., Itaú), others might require an in-person visit. Services like Infinity Pay are sometimes discouraged due to potential account blocking issues.
  • Nubank is a popular digital bank in Brazil for foreigners to open accounts without extensive physical documentation (unlike traditional banks). However, users report instances of sudden account blocks, suggesting that compliance checks are strict.
  • Opening a bank account in Brazil (e.g., Banco do Brasil, Nubank) typically requires a physical RNM card. Digital/temporary RNM documentation is often insufficient for online applications, resulting in processing errors (e.g., error code 904-000).
  • Opening a bank account in Brazil as a non-resident is difficult but possible. Most banks require RNM (Registro Nacional Migratório), but Banco Rendimento is an exception for non-residents with a CPF. Traditional banks like Banco do Brasil or Itaú have inconsistent policies depending on the branch and current manager; opening an account often requires a CPF and proof of rental agreement (contrato de aluguel). SWIFT transfers from Russia are non-functional; P2P crypto exchanges are the primary method for moving funds.
  • Opening a bank account in Brazil with only a foreign passport and CPF (Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas) is generally not possible. Specific requirements and procedures vary by bank, and often involve proving residency or other documentation beyond just a passport and CPF.
  • It is possible to open a bank account with Bradesco in Brazil using only a phone number and a CPF (Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas - Brazilian individual taxpayer registry number). Initially, a debit card for Pix transactions is issued. Subsequently, a multi-currency debit Visa card can be obtained via the Bradesco mobile application. However, success rates vary; some users report being denied. Visiting a bank branch in person with a reference (e.g., photo of a successfully opened card) might improve the chances.
  • CPF (Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas) is a mandatory individual tax ID in Brazil for residents and many non-residents. It can be obtained at Receita Federal offices (usually 30-60 minutes). Requirements typically include a valid passport, proof of address, and parents' full names. Electronic or printed versions are available. It is required for local SIM cards, bank accounts, and legal registration.
