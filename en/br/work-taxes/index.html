Work & Taxes: how it works in Brazil — first-hand

Real experience of people from chats on «Work & Taxes» in Brazil — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • Taxation for IT contractors (PJ - Pessoa Jurídica) in Brazil depends on the tax regime (Simples Nacional). Exporting services (serviços de exportação) can lead to exemptions on taxes like PIS, COFINS, and ISS, potentially reducing the effective tax rate to ~3-6%. Income taken as profit distribution (dividendos) is generally tax-exempt up to a certain monthly threshold. However, banking institutions often view dividend-based income as less stable than CLT (formal employment) when assessing credit/mortgage applications.
  • For migrants in Brazil seeking employment, various support channels exist, including migration aid funds and online communities. However, be cautious of 'helpers' charging exorbitant fees for basic tasks like filling out forms (e.g., CPF, SUS). Research and utilize free or affordable resources. Official platforms and community-shared information within expat groups can provide guidance on navigating bureaucratic processes and job searches without overpaying.
  • English teaching salaries in Brazil range from R$25-R$50 per hour in language schools and R$60-R$200 per hour for private tutors. The estimated monthly income ceiling for an English teacher is around R$4,000. This is comparable to salaries of teachers in some Russian regions, excluding additional bonuses. While teaching English can provide supplementary income, significant earnings are unlikely without substantial hours or additional work.
  • Naturalized citizens in Brazil must complete military registration ("alistamento militar") up to the age of 45. However, actual conscription only targets 18-year-olds, and there is a surplus of volunteers, so older applicants and most young expats are immediately placed in the reserve (receiving a "Certificado de Dispensa de Incorporação"). Failure to register results in severe civil restrictions: inability to obtain/renew a passport, enroll in public universities, or sign government contracts.
  • Cities in Brazil at higher altitudes, such as Curitiba and Belo Horizonte (around 900m), offer cooler climates. Campos do Jordão is at 1600m. Curitiba has an average temperature forecast of 22°C but requires travel to the coast. These cities can be considered for their size and infrastructure, appealing to those seeking urban environments with potential for business development.
  • During the COVID-19 pandemic in Brazil (2020-2022), restrictions varied by region and public spaces. General: Masks were often mandatory indoors (e.g., shops in Rio), recommended outdoors, but not strictly enforced by police in all areas. Vaccination was widely encouraged, but mandatory for specific activities (e.g., gym entry, university admission, travel to Fernando de Noronha). São Paulo: Experienced periods of severe lockdown with empty cities. Rio de Janeiro: Citizens were strict about mask-wearing in stores; beaches were closed and people were sometimes removed by police. Travel restrictions were implemented for some areas (e.g., Buzios required proof of residence). Rural/Remote Areas: Restrictions were often less strict or ignored, with people gathering without masks.

  • Brazil's tax residency rules are crucial for digital nomads. Generally, if you spend more than 183 days in Brazil within a 12-month period, you are considered a tax resident. This means you become liable for Brazilian taxes on your worldwide income. Obtaining a temporary visa (e.g., for digital nomads) can also establish tax residency from the visa's start date. It's essential to understand these implications and consult with a tax professional to ensure compliance with Brazilian tax laws regarding income earned remotely.

While obtaining a work visa is possible, it typically depends on having a job offer from a Brazilian company willing to sponsor it. The salary expectations should also be realistic; even for skilled labor, wages in Brazil can be significantly lower than in many other countries. For instance, a construction worker might earn around $2,000 USD per month, which is considered a good income in Brazil but may be below expectations for foreigners. Job searching platforms specific to Brazil should be explored, but language proficiency remains a primary barrier.

Climate reduces seasonal clothing and heating costs. However, perceived 'basic' housing might not meet expectations for comfort. For professional integration, however, achieving a proficient level of Portuguese is essential, although many words share Latin roots, potentially aiding learners.
