  • Naver accounts may trigger security locks when accessing from different IP addresses (traveling abroad). It is essential to keep offline screenshots of QR codes, tickets, and booking confirmations to ensure service access remains possible even if the account is temporarily restricted.
  • Legally immigrating to South Korea typically requires a valid work visa, which is usually sponsored by a South Korean company. This process is most feasible for highly skilled professionals in fields like IT, science, or modeling, or those with a Korean educational background. Finding employment with a Korean company that is willing to sponsor a visa is the primary step for legal relocation. Unofficial channels or promises of visa assistance without a legitimate job offer can lead to exploitation or precarious legal status.
  • This is a promotional message offering comprehensive visa application services for South Korea, including K-ETA, tourist visas, business visas, student visas, and ethnic visas (F-4). Services include university selection, document processing, embassy submissions, and post-arrival assistance like obtaining an ID card, SIM card, and bank account.   • Immigrating to South Korea as a family without specific qualifications (highly skilled worker, ethnic Korean) or an employer's sponsorship is difficult. While children may be admitted to universities, parents cannot legally reside or work there. Frequent visits are possible, but permanent relocation for parents is generally not feasible unless they are remote workers or can financially support frequent visits.
  • Working as a photographer in South Korea as a foreigner is challenging due to strict hiring regulations. A specific visa that legally permits opening a small business as a foreigner does not exist. Employers are unlikely to hire foreign photographers unless they possess an exceptional portfolio, and even then, a work visa (like E-series) or a specific permit would be required. Proficiency in Korean is also crucial for attracting potential employers.
  • Working in South Korea requires a specific visa that permits employment. A tourist visa or K-ETA does not grant the right to work. Individuals seeking employment opportunities should research and apply for the appropriate work visa category before traveling. Attempting to work on a tourist visa can lead to deportation and future entry bans.
