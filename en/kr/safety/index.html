Safety: how it works in South Korea — first-hand

Real experience of people from chats on «Safety» in South Korea — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • Users in Seoul report inconsistent weather, with some experiencing constant cloudiness and others sunburn in May. Advise travelers to pack both umbrellas and sunscreen. Sunscreen with SPF is recommended daily, regardless of sunshine. Two swimsuits were packed by one user but remained unused.
  • Importing cosmetics from South Korea to Russia/other countries: customs generally allow personal quantities. Avoid commercial-looking shipments (e.g., dozens of identical items). If carrying large quantities, distribute items among several travelers to avoid triggering customs inspections for commercial intent.
  • Jeju Island rainy season runs from late June through summer. Weather is highly variable. Wind-proof gear is recommended over umbrellas. Official beaches may have closed facilities (showers, lifeguards) off-season or during bad weather. Be aware of rip currents and tidal patterns.
  • Luggage wrapping services are available at Incheon International Airport. Check airport directories or information desks upon arrival for the nearest wrapping station location.
  • Dedicated smoking rooms within cafes are extremely rare in South Korea. Most establishments require patrons to step outside to designated smoking areas. Smoking facilities are typically found in PC bangs (internet cafes). Some pubs in areas like Itaewon, Gangnam, or Dongdaemun may have designated smoking areas, but these are exceptions. Be prepared to smoke outdoors when visiting most cafes.
  • Purchases from duty-free shops in Korea (liquids exceeding 100ml) can be taken as carry-on only if the packaging remains sealed and you have the receipt. Crucially, if transferring in a country like China (e.g., Beijing, Shanghai), transit security will confiscate these items if you leave the transit zone or if they require re-check-in of luggage. It is generally impossible to add items to checked luggage once in the sterile zone of the airport.
  • Entry requirements for South Korea: 1. Carry cash ($100 per day) as proof of funds. 2. Have printed hotel reservations and return tickets. 3. Prepare a detailed daily travel itinerary. 4. K-ETA details (occupation, purpose of visit) must be consistent with statements given at the immigration desk. 5. Animal products (meat/dairy) are strictly prohibited; customs may record incidents, even if no immediate fine is issued.
  • Airport security metal detectors usually trigger for bulky metal items like watches or belts. Small jewelry made of stainless steel or titanium often does not trigger alarms. If the alarm goes off, security staff typically uses a hand-held scanner for secondary screening.
  • General tourist medication in original packaging is typically allowed without issues. For liquids in carry-on: individual containers must not exceed 100ml, with a total volume not exceeding 1 liter per person. Always keep essential medications and liquids that might leak (like vape cartridges) in your carry-on luggage.
  • Registration for Kakao T (taxi app) often requires KakaoTalk integration. If verification SMS codes fail to arrive for non-Korean numbers, verify that the mobile operator supports international SMS delivery. A known workaround is to ensure the KakaoTalk account is fully verified first, then link it to Kakao T. Some users report success by selecting the 'foreigner' registration option within the app.
  • All lithium batteries, power banks, and electronic devices with built-in batteries must be carried in carry-on luggage. Checked luggage containing these items is often flagged during transit security, leading to confiscation or significant flight delays.
  • When processing tax-free refunds at Korean airports, expect a detailed inspection for high-value items (around $1,000 USD each), such as phones, lenses, or jewelry. These items must be kept in your carry-on luggage and presented to customs officials. For lower-value purchases like cosmetics, clothing, or souvenirs, inspections are generally less strict, and even multiple large luggage items are usually not an issue. The key is to ensure your total purchases do not appear to be a commercial quantity, which could raise questions.
  • During summer, Seoul experiences hot and humid weather, often around 30°C. Air conditioning in public transport (metro, KTX) and shopping malls can be quite strong, leading to significant temperature differences. Travelers are advised to wear light, breathable clothing (e.g., loose cotton pants, shorts, thin long-sleeve shirts for sun protection). It's recommended to carry a light jacket or long-sleeved top for cooler indoor environments or after rain. Sun protection is crucial; carry or purchase high-SPF sunscreen, and consider hats or umbrellas. Locals often use thin arm covers available in convenience stores (CU, 7/11) for sun protection.
  • Users may have issues with Woori Bank Pass card balance not reflecting in the app after a top-up. This can be due to display errors. Balance can be checked via the web version of the app or metro terminals. Using a Russian phone number for verification is possible.
  • Users may encounter difficulties when trying to verify their ID on Naver, even after uploading passport photos and completing forms. Some users report needing multiple attempts for successful verification. The 'Ask a question' section on Naver might be used to inquire about specific verification issues.
  • For transportation in Seoul, use Naver Map or Kakao Map as Google Maps has limitations. Purchase and top-up metro cards at machines; a 500 KRW deposit is refundable upon card return. Be aware that some accommodations, particularly 'love motels,' have check-in times starting at 10 PM. If items are lost in a taxi, the support service attempts to contact the driver within 24 hours, but responses may be delayed.
  • Smoking in South Korea is heavily restricted. Smoking is prohibited in most public areas. Dedicated smoking areas are often near convenience stores, marked by small tables and ashtrays, or clusters of locals smoking in secluded spots or alleys. Do not smoke in non-designated areas to avoid fines.

  • Hand luggage liquid regulations: individual containers must not exceed 100ml. Total liquid volume per passenger is limited to 1 liter. All liquids must be carried in a transparent, re-sealable bag.
  • Essential entry requirements for South Korea to avoid deportation: 1. Carry physical cash (min $100/day). 2. Have confirmed accommodation bookings for the entire duration of stay. 3. Prepare a detailed tourist itinerary (3 locations per day, logical geographic clustering). 4. Ensure K-ETA details match the actual purpose of the trip (do not select 'Tourism' if visiting friends; use 'Visit' or mention the contact). 5. If questioned, provide direct, consistent answers; avoid mentioning 'friends' if you lack their full contact details or if they are also tourists, as this triggers suspicion of illegal work.
  • Purchasing Apple devices in South Korea: 1. Prices are competitive with Russia, but Tax Free refunds significantly reduce the final cost. 2. Device compatibility is universal. 3. Camera shutter sound on iPhones is determined by the region/country associated with the Apple ID, not the hardware's purchase location.
  • Smoking in public areas in South Korea can be challenging due to a lack of designated ashtrays and visible smokers. It is advisable to seek out designated smoking areas or smoke in alleys, particularly in residential zones. While smoking might be more common on bustling streets in the evening, there's a risk of being reported to the authorities by locals. Therefore, exercising caution and smoking only where explicitly permitted or where others are smoking is recommended.
  • Korean Air's carry-on baggage policy enforcement can vary. Minor size discrepancies are often overlooked, and passengers may be allowed to board. However, strict checks are possible, leading to the checked baggage option with associated fees. Fee amounts are route-dependent and can be confirmed via the airline's website or the original booking platform.
  • Dress codes in South Korea are generally informal and liberal; tourists can wear open shoulders, mini-skirts, or casual clothes without judgment. However, sun protection is advised due to strong UV rays, and light layers are recommended for indoor air conditioning.
  • Importing food items into South Korea is subject to customs regulations aimed at preventing the introduction of pests and diseases. Travelers must declare all food products. Specific items, especially fresh produce and meat products, may be prohibited or require phytosanitary certificates. It is advisable to check the official guidelines of the Korea Customs Service or the Animal and Plant Quarantine Agency (APQA) for a comprehensive list of restricted items and declaration procedures.
  • The term 'personal item' in flight baggage allowances typically refers to items like a handbag or a small backpack that passengers can carry in addition to their cabin baggage and checked luggage. This item is usually included within the overall baggage allowance and does not represent an extra charge or separate category unless specified otherwise by the airline.
  • Sun protection is a high priority in South Korea, leading many individuals to wear full-coverage clothing, even in cooler weather or by the sea. This includes specialized suits, hats, and umbrellas to prevent sunburn and UV exposure. Beachgoers often opt for dark, full-body attire for maximum protection, a practice rooted in prioritizing skin health.
  • If your name is incorrectly read during Nol World verification, contact support for assistance. Deleting and re-registering might be an option, but it may not always work if the name has already been verified. Data is reportedly stored for six months.
  • Power banks are permitted in carry-on luggage on flights to South Korea via Beijing. Ensure power banks have the CCC marking, as checks can be strict. Power banks must be transported in carry-on luggage.
  • Aerosol transport: always pack spray canisters in checked luggage to avoid security disposal. While hand luggage rules may theoretically allow small containers (under 100ml), local security checkpoints (e.g., Beijing subway or airport security) frequently confiscate them regardless of volume.
  • South Korean beaches have strict lifeguard protocols. Swimming is often prohibited during strong winds or waves. Official swimming seasons are limited. Swimming outside of designated areas or hours can lead to warnings from lifeguards and potential fines. Early September is often cited as a better time for swimming as official crowds thin out.
  • In case of a lost child in South Korea, the recommended action is to stay in a visible area under CCTV coverage and contact the police. Police response is reliable. Essential safety preparation includes providing the child with physical copies of contact information (hotel address, parent's phone number). Digital communication via KakaoTalk is highly recommended as it remains functional without a VPN.
  • Transferring money from Russia to South Korea via KoronaPay (Zolotaya Korona) is possible, with Ozon Bank mentioned as a working option. Cashing out transfers may require using services like Hanpass/GME. Be cautious of individuals offering direct currency exchange via private messages, as they may be scammers. Receiving funds directly to a bank card is also an option.
  • Smoking in South Korea is strictly regulated. Only smoke in designated smoking areas or where local residents are observed smoking, even if 'No Smoking' signs are nearby (which often indicate the absence of surveillance cameras). Smoking in unauthorized public areas can result in fines.
  • An attendee asked about the age restriction for a concert in Seoul with an official age limit of 16+. They were concerned about bringing a 12-year-old with a parent. Despite the age restriction on the ticket, the 12-year-old was allowed entry with a parent.
  • Taxi scams in South Korea include intentional route extension and manual fare surcharges added at the end of a trip. Passengers should use GPS navigation (Google Maps) to monitor the route and verify the meter fare.

  • Bringing cosmetics and clothing from South Korea to Russia for personal use typically does not raise concerns at customs, even with significant quantities. Travelers have reported successfully bringing large amounts of cosmetics or multiple large suitcases (up to 23 kg each) without facing customs inquiries or issues. The assumption is that these items are for personal consumption rather than commercial resale.
  • If an item is lost in a taxi, the service attempts to contact the driver within 24 hours. However, retrieval may not be immediate, and drivers typically do not offer free return services even if identified. Passengers should contact the taxi company's support for assistance.
  • To minimize entry risks in South Korea: 1. Carry physical cash (approx. $100 USD/day). 2. Have confirmed return flight tickets and hotel bookings. 3. Prepare a detailed day-by-day travel itinerary. 4. Ensure K-ETA details match actual travel data. Do not present these documents unless asked; have them ready in case of inspection.
  • Smoking is highly restricted in South Korea, with limited designated smoking areas. Smoking is often prohibited in alleyways and near establishments. While some locals disregard these rules, it is advisable for visitors to adhere to regulations to avoid potential fines or penalties. Designated smoking zones with ashtrays are the safest option, often found in residential complexes or specific public areas.
  • Sharks are not typically found in the designated swimming areas of Incheon beaches. While sightings might occur on undeveloped or wild beaches, official swimming zones are generally considered safe.
  • Hand luggage weight and personal item restrictions are enforced at Incheon Airport (ICN), though enforcement can vary by airline and individual staff. Minor overweight allowances (1-2 kg) are sometimes permitted, but it is advisable to adhere strictly to airline regulations to avoid having items removed or discarded. Checking with the specific airline regarding their policies is recommended.
  • Waste sorting in South Korea is meticulous. While hotels often handle sorting internally, residents in apartments or guesthouses are expected to sort waste themselves. Key categories include plastic bottles, paper, glass, and cardboard, all separated. Plastic bottles should have labels removed, and caps collected separately. It's also common practice to rinse containers. Some areas have specific guidelines, such as separating lids from bottles. Hotels typically manage the sorting process for guests.
  • Smoking in South Korea is heavily regulated with designated smoking areas being scarce. Fines can be issued for smoking in prohibited public spaces, including near subways, bus stops, and crosswalks, especially if surveillance cameras are present or a complaint is filed. While locals sometimes disregard signs, tourists should be cautious. Some apartment complexes have designated smoking zones. Foreigners with long-term visas or residency may face stricter enforcement and potential impacts on visa status or citizenship applications if fined.
  • South Korea utilizes specialized recycling bins, often designated for beverage containers such as bottles and cans. These bins facilitate waste sorting and recycling efforts. It is important to dispose of waste in the appropriate bins to comply with local regulations and maintain cleanliness.
  • The weather in South Korea during July and August is characterized by intense heat and humidity, often exacerbated by rain. Outdoor activities can be very uncomfortable, feeling like a 'bath.' Many locals and residents seek refuge in air-conditioned indoor spaces. The natural scenery is less appealing than in spring or autumn. Traveling to coastal areas like Donghae or Sokcho might be an option, but the sea can be rough, limiting swimming. Indoor attractions are a more comfortable alternative during this period.
  • When traveling to South Korea, it is generally not mandatory to bring prescriptions for common medications. However, for controlled substances like neuroleptics and normothymics, it is highly recommended to carry a prescription, preferably translated into English. This can help clarify the purpose of the medication to customs officials if questioned.
  • Street ash trays in South Korea are often designed with wet wipes on top. This feature serves to extinguish cigarette butts, prevent fires, and minimize ash dispersal. Similar innovations exist in other countries, such as water-filled ash trays in Japan or specialized powders used in China.
  • The swimming season in South Korea typically begins in July. Popular beach destinations like Jeju Island and Gangneung are suitable for swimming. Busan's waters may still be cool in early July. While locals might opt for full-coverage swimwear (like rash guards), tourists can wear standard swimsuits without issue. Swimming beyond designated safety buoys is prohibited and may result in being asked to return to shore.
