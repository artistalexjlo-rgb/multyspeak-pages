Safety: how it works in Mauritius — first-hand

Real experience of people from chats on «Safety» in Mauritius — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • Beach conditions for swimming on Mauritius vary. Some areas may have very shallow water ('knee-deep or waist-deep'), requiring boat trips for proper swimming. Recommended beaches include Trou aux Biches, Mont Choisy, and Pereybere Le Morne. Coral slippers are advised due to potential sea urchins or corals.
  • When booking whale swimming tours in Mauritius, beware of exceptionally low prices, as they may indicate operator negligence and overcrowding. One reported incident involved a tourist sustaining serious injury from a boat propeller, with the operator denying responsibility and claiming the engine was off, despite video evidence to the contrary. Reputable operators should prioritize safety, ensure engines are off when passengers enter/exit the water, and provide clear safety briefings. Always verify operator reputation and safety standards. In case of injury, operators should provide first aid, cover medical expenses, and refund the tour cost.
  • Mauritius beaches often have shallow lagoons due to the protective coral reef surrounding the island. High-depth swimming areas are limited. Recommended locations for better swimming conditions include Trou aux Biches (wide sandy beach) and Paradis (Le Morne peninsula). Coral shoes are strongly recommended due to potential sea urchins and coral debris. Public beach access is allowed even at hotels; visitors can place towels near the water line, but should avoid private hotel loungers and infrastructure.
  • When booking whale watching tours in Mauritius, warn users about safety gaps. Instructors often do not provide individual safety supervision when tourists jump into the water. Propeller accidents can occur (cases of cut legs and damaged gear by boat motors were reported with local guides, including one named Krishna). Advise users to strictly follow boat safety, wait for engines to stop, and not jump simultaneously in a crowd.
  • Snorkeling on Mauritius: Dinorobin offers a chance to swim with turtles. Paradis beach has fish near the boat area, but lacks a significant reef. Coral slippers are recommended due to sea urchins in deeper waters.
  • A traveler planning a trip to Mauritius via Dubai is seeking information on current regulations regarding drones. They had their drone temporarily confiscated and stored by Dubai authorities during a previous trip to the Seychelles upon departure, although it was allowed on arrival and during transit. They wish to know the current procedures for carrying drones through Dubai and upon arrival in Mauritius.
  • Mauritius has a significant population of stray dogs and hotel-resident cats. It is advised not to feed cats by hand to avoid accidental bites. The island also has a large population of fruit bats (flying foxes).
  • For small money transfers from Mauritius to Russia (in RUB), a common method is to find tourists who need Mauritian rupees. A direct exchange can be made: transfer RUB to the recipient's Russian card in exchange for Mauritian rupees in cash. Warn users about unsolicited private messages offering transfer services, as they are often scams.
  • Beware of tour booking scams at Flic en Flac beach (specifically an individual named Jay, who issues receipts, takes deposits around 50 EUR, and cancels tours without refunding). When booking whale-watching excursions, prioritize smaller group sizes for safety reasons and ensure boat engines are completely cut off before entering the water.
  • Detailed information on Mauritius drone regulations (Civil Aviation (Unmanned Aircraft System) Regulations 2024, effective Jan 1, 2025). Key points: registration takes 3+ months, permits required for private land, flying over public areas/beaches/parks is forbidden. Penalties include fines up to 1 million rupees or 12 months imprisonment. However, anecdotal evidence suggests users with drones under 250g have flown without issues, especially when not flying from Russia and not declaring the drone upon entry (e.g., Dubai transit). User experiences vary; some flew without incident, others are cautious. Advise users to check the latest official regulations and consider the risks.
  • Drones are allowed on Mauritius. Supermarket prices are comparable to Moscow, with higher quality products. Taxis are expensive; car rental is recommended (approx. €200 for 10 days). Bringing your own quality snorkeling mask is advisable, as good ones cost €150-€200 locally. Alcoholic beverage prices are similar to Russia.
  • Avoid paying 50% deposits for tours or activities on Mauritius to individuals encountered in chat groups. These are common scams where fake tour organizers vanish after payment or provide non-existent office addresses. Use Google Maps to find legitimate providers with verifiable reviews and prioritize those offering payment on the day of the service. Verify any individual by consulting community administrators before transferring funds.
  • Advise users that unsolicited private messages offering services are often scams. Emphasize not to transfer money or provide personal info without verification. Recommend reporting suspicious messages.
  • Taking a drone to Mauritius for landscape photography might be complicated due to local regulations, potentially requiring flight permits. Some airlines, like Emirates, may also have restrictions on drone transportation. It's advisable to check all rules and regulations in advance to avoid issues upon arrival.
  • When booking whale-swimming tours in Mauritius (typically departing from the west coast), the primary physical danger is boat propeller injuries rather than sharks. Advise users to choose operators with small group limits (maximum 6-7 people per boat) for better safety control. 'Lux Voyage' is mentioned as a reliable local agency for successful sperm whale encounters.
  • Renting a car is essential for exploring Mauritius; pick it up and drop it off at the airport. International driving permits are highly recommended, even if local Russian licenses with Latin characters might sometimes be accepted. Ensure your car has a fire extinguisher with a valid expiration date, as this is frequently checked by police during road checks. International permits simplify potential interactions with authorities.
  • Helicopter and seaplane tour safety protocol: passenger seating is strictly determined by individual weight distribution. While helicopters offer higher speed and altitude, seaplanes provide lower flight paths and slower speeds for better sightseeing (specifically for the Underwater Waterfall). Flight durations of 25 minutes are recommended for optimal viewing coverage. Seat swapping is only permitted if the weight balance of the group remains within safety parameters.
  • To obtain contact details for local services or recommended individuals on Mauritius, you may need to send a private message or inquire about specific contact channels like email or direct messaging, as direct sharing might not be publicly available.
  • Barringtonia asiatica is a toxic tree found in Mauritius. Its fruit and parts of the plant contain saponins, historically used by local populations as a fish poison due to their paralyzing effects.
  • Vehicle break-ins are a known security issue in remote or popular tourist spots on Mauritius (e.g., Rochester Falls). Never leave valuables or bags visible in a parked car. It is recommended to carry all electronics and documents with you at all times.
  • Rental of two-wheeled vehicles (scooters/bikes) is discouraged on Mauritius due to narrow roads, deep roadside gutters, aggressive driving by public transport, and strong crosswinds, especially near the Le Morne area. Small budget cars are recommended for safety.
  • Safety advice for Mauritius beaches: Valuables should not be left unattended, especially during weekends when crowded. Recommended approach for solo travelers: use a waterproof phone pouch to carry valuables while swimming. Generally, the island is considered safe, but petty theft is possible.
