Housing: how it works in Germany — first-hand

Real experience of people from chats on «Housing» in Germany — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • When booking flights with short layovers, especially with children, verify the connection times after any schedule changes. A 45-minute layover in Antalya, even on a single ticket with Pegasus, can be extremely difficult, particularly when dealing with luggage retrieval (like strollers) and young children. If the next available flight is significantly later, and onward travel (like a train) is booked, this can lead to major disruptions. Contacting the airline for rebooking options or accommodation is crucial, though responses can be slow.
  • In Germany, moisture control is crucial for preventing mold (Schimmel). Use dehumidifiers (Entfeuchter), ensure proper ventilation, and report severe mold issues to the landlord immediately. Tenants may be held financially liable for mold damage if negligence is proven.
  • Rental market in Germany is highly competitive. Remote long-term rentals are non-existent. Tenants should use platforms like Immowelt, prepare a 'candidate portfolio' (cover letter, proof of income, Schufa score if available, or enrollment certificate), and consider temporary housing (Airbnb/Wunderflats) before searching locally.
  • When communicating with landlords in Germany regarding apartment handovers or security deposit (Kaution) returns, messaging apps are not legally binding. Official notices must be sent via registered physical mail (Einschreiben) to serve as legal proof. If the landlord unjustifiably delays returning the deposit, tenants can initiate an online summary court procedure for debt collection (Mahnverfahren/Mahnbescheid), which is highly effective and usually forces a prompt return of funds.
  • When searching for accommodation in Germany, utilize established platforms such as Immoscout24, Immowelt, and Kleinanzeigen for long-term rentals. For short-term stays (e.g., one month), look for 'Ferienwohnung' (holiday apartments) or explore platforms like Wonderflats. Be aware that 'WG' (Wohngemeinschaft) refers to shared apartments where rooms are rented, with common kitchen and bathroom facilities, distinct from entire apartment rentals.
  • In Germany, a 'Mietkautionsversicherung' (rental deposit insurance) is an alternative to a traditional cash deposit (Kaution). Instead of paying the deposit directly to the landlord, the tenant pays a premium to an insurance company, which then acts as a guarantor for the deposit amount. This prevents the landlord from independently accessing the funds. However, not all landlords accept this form of deposit, with many preferring a direct cash payment. Tariffs for Mietkautionsversicherung can be compared on platforms like Check24.
  • Students in Germany can find housing through 'Gastfamilie' (host family, often including meals) or 'Zwischenmiete' (subletting a room from another student who is away for a semester). These options can offer cultural immersion and cost savings compared to traditional rentals.
  • Registering an address (Anmeldung) at a location where one does not actually reside is illegal in Germany and can lead to fines of up to 50,000 euros for both the individual and the person providing the false registration. In some cases, multiple registrations might be possible, but actual residence is key.
  • German citizens, especially late repatriates or digital nomads, must be aware of health insurance and tax residency rules. Compulsory health insurance is mandatory for residents. Private health insurance costs range from €350-€800/month for a 40-year-old and requires an annual gross income of approximately €74,000 to qualify. Switching from private to public insurance is difficult after age 50-57. ADAC offers medical insurance. Residency is typically established by spending over 180 days per year in Germany, which mandates registration (Anmeldung), GEZ payments (broadcasting fee), and tax obligations. Failure to comply can lead to significant penalties from the Finanzamt. Registration is also required for purchasing and registering a car in Germany to obtain German license plates. Cash payments for cars from dealerships are usually limited to €10,000; private sales might be more flexible but pose risks. 'Incoming-Versicherung' (temporary insurance) for €80-€100/month is an option for temporary stays (up to 5 years) but not for German citizens or for extending residence permits.
  • For short-term stays (e.g., 6 months for language courses) in Germany when international bank cards are unavailable: 1. Use wg-gesucht.de to find student sublets (Zwischenmiete), which are often furnished. 2. Contact serviced apartments (like Brera Serviced Apartments) which offer Anmeldung (registration). 3. As a community hack, local Russian Orthodox churches can sometimes assist with temporary housing, local contacts, or cash-to-card payment facilitation for bookings.
  • Finding rental apartments in Berlin is highly competitive. The market has shifted from readily available 'nachmieter' listings to a scenario where direct listings on platforms like ImmoScout24 often result in rejections. Real estate agents ('Makler') are available but typically focus on higher-income clients. Prospective tenants should be prepared for a challenging search, with potential landlords scrutinizing income (often requiring rent to be no more than one-third of net income) and other factors.
  • Late resettlers (§7/§8) in Germany experiencing a refusal of JobCenter benefits or housing costs for their children due to delays in obtaining residence permits from the Ausländerbehörde should seek support and advice within specialized online communities or legal aid services focused on late resettlers. This indicates a known systemic challenge rather than an isolated incident.
  • For marriage registration intended for German family reunification, consider alternative locations like Georgia or Kazakhstan if German procedures are complex. Be aware that the country of marriage can impact German authorities' requirements. Specifically, inquire about the need for a certificate of marital status from the foreign registry office, as this can influence the required documentation for reunification in Germany. Notarized translators are typically required, and while some documents might be translated from scans, in-person submission at the German registry office (Standesamt) is usually necessary. The process for obtaining a marriage license in Germany can be lengthy, involving obtaining permission from the court and a visa for the fiancé(e).
  • If you cannot secure an earlier appointment for your 'Anmeldung' (registration) in Germany within 14 days of arrival, it is generally permissible to schedule it for a later date. One user reported successfully registering an appointment a month after their arrival date without issues.
  • When listing an item on Kleinanzeigen (formerly eBay Kleinanzeigen), it may not appear immediately in your profile or search results. Listings usually become visible after a short verification period or may be subject to categorization and display algorithms. Check the 'My Ads' section for confirmation and allow some time for visibility.
  • In Germany, registering two residences (Hauptwohnsitz and Nebenwohnsitz) can trigger tax scrutiny. Some municipalities (like Chemnitz mentioned here) levy a secondary residence tax (Zweitwohnsitzsteuer). If a person claims their main residence is in one city but their work is in another, authorities might investigate to ensure the declared 'center of vital interests' (Mittelpunkt der Lebensinteressen) is accurate to prevent tax evasion.
  • Marriages performed abroad, particularly in countries like Georgia or the UAE, are generally recognized in Germany, even for German citizens. This approach is often chosen to avoid the extensive bureaucracy of marrying within Germany. It's crucial to ensure the marriage certificate is properly legalized for German authorities (e.g., apostille). However, any subsequent divorce proceedings for couples residing in Germany will typically be governed by German family law, regardless of where the marriage took place. German divorce laws can have significant financial implications for both parties.
  • In Germany, upon moving, inform the GEZ (Beitragsservice) of your new address. Your initial registration creates a household number. After moving, update your address online, even if your new registration (Anmeldung) is pending. The household is already registered and only requires an address change.
  • When purchasing package tours from Germany with a Belarusian passport, particularly for travel originating from Poland and booked through a German tour operator, potential difficulties may arise during hotel check-in. Hotel policies can vary, and some may impose additional charges if they perceive the booking as originating from a market with lower pricing than the customer's country of residence. Presenting proof of residency, such as a German residence permit (e.g., Aufenthaltstitel or Niederlassungserlaubnis), can help mitigate these issues.
  • Rental market advice: focus on management companies (Wohnungsbaugesellschaften) rather than private landlords for pet-friendly apartments. German apartments are typically rented unfurnished ('kalt'). Always verify pet policy in the lease contract before signing.
  • A change of residence by the German spouse (especially an inter-city or inter-state move) during a family reunion visa application process can significantly delay visa issuance. Documents may be misdirected to the old address, and the application must be transferred between the respective Ausländerbehörde (ABH), restarting processing. This can add 2-3 weeks or more to the waiting time. Applicants are advised to proactively contact the ABH in such situations.
