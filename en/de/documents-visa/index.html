Documents & Visa: how it works in Germany — first-hand

Real experience of people from chats on «Documents & Visa» in Germany — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • For a minor (16 years old) traveling alone from Russia to Germany (e.g., via Kazakhstan), a notarized consent for independent travel from one parent is sufficient. The consent must list all countries the child plans to visit. There are no specific rules restricting minors from taking night flights.
  • Entry into Germany with a non-EU visa and a European residence permit (issued after January 1st) requires careful attention to passport type and visa validity. National visa D, particularly for study or work, might allow travel within the EU, but official confirmation and specific country regulations should be checked. Biometric passports are strictly enforced for visas issued after January 1st, 2026.
  • To pursue a Master's degree in Architecture in Germany, a strong command of the German language is essential, typically at least B2 level, preferably C1, with specialized vocabulary relevant to the profession. While some programs may be taught in English, proficiency in German is often critical, especially for programs requiring close interaction with the professional field. Austria might have slightly lower language requirements, but German proficiency remains crucial. Admission criteria generally include academic transcripts (with a good average grade, ideally below 2.5) and a portfolio showcasing previous architectural projects and work. Prospective students should research specific university requirements for language proficiency and portfolio submission.
  • Pet import requirements to Germany (EU): 1. Microchip (must be implanted before or at the same time as rabies vaccination). 2. Valid rabies vaccination. 3. Antibody titration test (wait 30 days after vaccination before drawing blood). 4. Mandatory 3-month waiting period from the date of blood draw for the titration test. 5. Animal Health Certificate issued by a state veterinarian before travel. Titration results are valid for the animal's lifetime if rabies vaccinations remain uninterrupted.
  • When departing from Berlin airports with children of school age (e.g., 9 and 15 years old), authorities may inquire about their school attendance. It is advisable to carry documentation, such as a school-issued certificate, to explain the absence (e.g., school holidays, planned trip, or other valid reasons). Presenting such a document can facilitate a smooth departure process.

  • A Schengen visa issued by one country (e.g., Bulgaria) is primarily intended for entry into that country first, even though it grants access to the entire Schengen area. Attempting to enter another Schengen country (e.g., Germany) directly from a third country (e.g., Russia) without first entering the issuing country (Bulgaria) can lead to problems. Airlines at the point of departure or during a layover may deny boarding due to potential violation of the 'first entry rule,' or immigration authorities at the destination may deny entry. It is strongly advised to enter the Schengen area through the country that issued the visa.

If the visa was issued after this date, entry using the old passport is not allowed. Furthermore, if the Schengen visa is not issued by Germany (e.g., from a Southern European country) and one attempts to enter Germany, there is a risk of being accused of 'visa shopping,' which can lead to further complications. It's advisable to use the visa for the country that issued it to avoid issues.
  • Travel within the EU using a German national visa (Visa D) after registration in Germany is a point of confusion. While some sources suggest it allows travel within the Schengen Area, others state it is only for entry into Germany. Official legal confirmation is needed for definitive guidance.
  • To obtain a German residence permit (Aufenthaltstitel) under Section 18a (skilled worker with vocational training), B1 German language proficiency is typically required.
