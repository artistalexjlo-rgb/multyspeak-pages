Banking & Money in France: real experience from chats

Real experience of people from chats on «Banking & Money» in France — as it is, no fluff. For your case — ask Luky.

From real experience

  • Free Mobile often terminates eSim contracts (status: 'Cette ligne est résiliée') if terms of service are breached, frequently triggered by non-EU payment methods or suspected account misuse. If payment fails or cards are rejected, try alternative banking providers, but be aware that reactivation of a terminated line is usually impossible.
  • To travel from Nice to Saint-Tropez in France, the most cost-effective method involves taking a train to Saint-Raphaël and then transferring to a bus. This route is recommended for budget-conscious travelers. Additionally, day trips from Nice to various coastal towns, including Saint-Tropez, are feasible, with three days potentially being sufficient for Saint-Tropez itself, allowing for exploration of other nearby locations.
  • When renting a car from Sixt in France, confirm if a physical credit card in the renter's name is mandatory, especially if using a card belonging to someone else or a virtual card. Inquire about specific requirements for additional drivers and payment methods.
  • Opening a bank account and obtaining a bank card in France as a Russian citizen without a residency permit (WHS) is extremely difficult. Most French banks require proof of long-term stay and a valid reason for opening an account. Even with a Visa D, opening an account with online banks might be possible, but it depends on the ability to regularly fund the account. The online bank Nickel is often cited as a potential option for newcomers, but it has its own limitations.
  • Turkish bank cards, particularly those operating on the Troy payment system or issued by sanctioned entities like DenizBank, suffer from high rejection rates in European POS terminals. Travelers are advised to rely on international VISA/Mastercard systems or carry sufficient physical cash. Virtual cards linked to Apple/Google Pay are not a guaranteed solution if the underlying bank infrastructure is flagged by European acquirers.
  • A spouse may be able to join the primary visa holder in France on a visitor visa after the primary visa holder has established their status under the 'Profession Liberale' (PL) visa and opened a micro-enterprise. The primary visa holder's income (savings exceeding 5x SMIC) can serve as financial sponsorship. While the PL visa itself may not have a direct fast-track family reunification process, the visitor visa route for the spouse is a potential option. It is recommended to gather a comprehensive dossier and consult relevant forums, such as 'infrance su forum', for detailed guidance on visitor visa applications and requirements.
  • When using Euros in France, any valid Euro banknotes (old or new series) are accepted. However, it's highly recommended to carry smaller denominations (5, 10, 20, 50 EUR) for several reasons: 1. **Minimum Purchase Limits for Card Payments:** Many establishments, especially smaller shops or for basic purchases, have a minimum transaction amount (often €3-€5, sometimes up to €20) for card payments. Without small bills, you might be forced to pay cash or buy more than intended. 2. **Acceptance of Large Bills:** While not strictly illegal, large denominations like €500 notes are rarely encountered in circulation and may not be accepted by merchants due to concerns about counterfeiting and the difficulty of making change. It can be challenging to exchange them. 3. **Practicality:** Small bills are more convenient for everyday expenses, public transport tickets, and smaller purchases, avoiding the need to break large notes. While old series Euro notes are acceptable, new series notes are more common. There is no significant price difference or advantage in using old versus new notes for transactions within the Eurozone.
  • When leaving France, there are no strictly defined limits on the amount of cash that can be carried, with the general guideline being 'for personal needs.' However, customs officials may question amounts exceeding €1000. It is advisable to carry minimal cash for border control purposes. USD is generally not subject to scrutiny. It is recommended to exchange currency after passing through border control. Carrying excessive cash in European currencies without proper declaration or justification may lead to issues.
  • Users in Russia may face issues paying for Free Mobile services using foreign cards like Bybit. Explore alternative payment methods or contact Free Mobile support for solutions. Be aware that Free Mobile might throttle speeds in the second month of use, especially on unlimited plans, if significant data is consumed while roaming. Consider switching to a lower-data plan if this occurs.
  • Opening a bank account in France as a Russian citizen with a 'visa talent' can be challenging. Banks may be hesitant. Recommended strategies include: initiating conversation with pleasantries rather than immediately stating nationality, praising the bank, and seeking advice. It's suggested to try multiple bank branches, and if rejected, request a written refusal and escalate to the Central Bank of France. As an alternative, neo-banks like Nickel are mentioned as potentially viable options. The general advice is to be persistent and strategic.
  • The functionality of virtual cards, such as 'Plati po miru', when used in France depends on the card issuer and the payment network it operates on (Visa, Mastercard, etc.). Key considerations: 1. **Network Compatibility:** Ensure the virtual card is affiliated with a major international payment network (Visa, Mastercard) that is widely accepted in France. 2. **Merchant Acceptance:** While many large retailers, supermarkets, and public transport systems (like the Paris Metro) accept card payments, some smaller establishments might have minimum transaction limits or only accept cash (see related advice on minimum purchase limits). 3. **Online vs. In-Store:** Virtual cards can often be used for online purchases (e.g., booking tickets) or added to mobile payment systems (like Apple Pay or Google Pay) for in-store transactions, provided the merchant's terminals support contactless payments. 4. **Issuer's Policies:** Check with the provider of the virtual card for any specific restrictions or geographical limitations on their use abroad. **Recommendation:** It is advisable to test the virtual card for a small purchase initially (e.g., topping up a transport card or a small item in a shop) to confirm its functionality in France.
  • A Free Mobile customer in France is experiencing unexpected charges of €80 per month instead of the expected €19.99 for their plan. Invoices are not appearing in their account, and store representatives suggested the account might have been compromised. The advice given is to monitor bank statements for unauthorized direct debits (prélèvement) from Free Mobile and to block them if they are not recognized. Contacting Free Mobile support, possibly with the help of a French speaker, is recommended. Blocking the direct debit ('prélèvement') via the bank account is suggested as a protective measure against unknown charges.
  • Exchanging currency in France is generally unfavorable due to high commissions. It is more cost-effective to obtain EUR in the home country or use cross-border banking transfers to non-sanctioned bank cards. Always verify physical cash availability in bank branches before visiting.
  • When traveling to France with cash, travelers must adhere to currency declaration rules: 1. **Declaration Threshold:** You are required to declare cash (including banknotes, coins, traveler's checks, money orders, and certain other monetary instruments) if the total amount is equal to or exceeds €10,000 upon entering or leaving France. 2. **Transit:** These rules also apply to cash carried when transiting through France, even if you do not formally enter the country (e.g., staying airside at an airport). 3. **Source of Funds:** While the primary requirement is declaration above €10,000, customs officials have the authority to ask for the source of funds for any amount, especially if they deem it necessary. 4. **Consequences of Non-Compliance:** Failure to declare cash can result in significant penalties, including fines and confiscation of the undeclared funds. 5. **Procedure:** Undeclared cash can be declared at customs upon arrival or departure. It is advisable to consult the official French customs website ('Douanes') or contact them directly for the most precise and up-to-date information regarding declaration procedures and specific requirements.
  • Using 500 EUR banknotes in Paris can be difficult at small merchants. They can be spent successfully either in luxury retail stores/outlets during major purchases or at automated self-checkout machines in large supermarkets (like Auchan/Carrefour), which accept high-denomination notes and provide change.
  • Incidents of cashiers short-changing customers have been reported in France (and Italy). If a customer suspects they have been given incorrect change, they should calmly but firmly point out the error to the cashier. If the cashier denies it, the customer should politely but insistently ask to speak with a manager (`responsable`). This action often leads to the correct change being issued. Direct punishment of the cashier is unlikely, but getting the correct amount back is usually possible. This tactic relies on customer inattention, language barriers, or haste.
  • When paying for fuel at French gas stations using a card, be aware that a temporary deposit (e.g., 100-150 EUR) may be pre-authorized on your card. This amount is usually released within a few days after the actual fuel cost is deducted, but delays can occur. Using cards like Revolut might result in faster deposit release.
  • Exporting large amounts of cash (Euros) from France to Russia is subject to strict regulations and may be prohibited or significantly limited due to European sanctions. While declaring the amount and providing proof of origin (e.g., employment contract) is generally recommended for large sums, it may not guarantee successful transit of Euros. Dollars might be more permissible. Utilizing cryptocurrency is suggested as an alternative for transferring funds, but caution is advised for those unfamiliar with it. Exchanging Euros for Rubles directly from France to a Russian account is another option, but official exchange channels should be used. Be aware that Russian banks may have restrictions on accepting older or damaged Euro banknotes.
  • Traveler is concerned about carrying USD versus EUR when entering France. Advised that USD might be less problematic than EUR, with some anecdotal evidence of EUR being confiscated at certain borders (though this may be specific to Eastern European borders). Suggestion is to carry minimal EUR for small purchases and USD for other needs, explaining the purpose if questioned. For language barriers, a translation app on the phone is recommended as most French officials can communicate in English, or a translation can be used if needed.
  • Belarusian bank cards may experience intermittent acceptance issues in France. If NFC (tap-to-pay) fails, inserting the card into the POS terminal and entering a PIN code often resolves the transaction. If it continues to fail, waiting 24 hours can often resolve temporary acquiring bank blocks.
  • Technobank cards (presumed to be from Belarus) have been successfully used for payments in France. A user reported multiple successful transactions this year, including at ticket terminals, and a large payment of nearly 2,000 EUR via a tax office terminal in the previous year. This indicates good acceptance of Technobank cards within France's payment infrastructure.
  • Free Mobile's eSIM registration requires a French IBAN. Users bypass this restriction by using an online French IBAN generator to complete the registration form, while paying for the actual service using standard international debit/credit cards.
  • For Belarusian citizens in France, the compatibility of bank cards varies. Alfa-Bank cards reportedly stopped working in November. Zepter Bank cards were functional in March 2026, with only a few minor establishments not accepting them. Users are advised to share their experiences with specific Belarusian bank cards in France.
  • Be cautious of potential scams when paying with cash in French establishments. Instances of incorrect change being given or being overcharged for orders have been reported. It is advisable to always check your receipt and count your change carefully. If you suspect an error or overcharge, assertively request a correction or refund. Understanding the local language can be beneficial in resolving such issues. Some advise being firm with cashiers and vendors to prevent them from attempting to overcharge.
